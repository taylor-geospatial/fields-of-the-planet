\begin{abstract}
Existing field-boundary benchmarks use 10\,m Sentinel-2 imagery, which approaches the resolution floor for smallholder parcels. We pair every patch in the \emph{Fields of The World} (FTW) benchmark~\cite{kerner2024ftw} with a co-registered 3\,m PlanetScope image and a freshly rasterized 3-class label, and call the result \textbf{FTW-HD}: 25 countries, 133{,}168 (patch, window) pairs, built from $\sim$6{,}100 PlanetScope scenes accessed via HTTP range reads against Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFFs in $\sim$17\,h on a single commodity CPU partition.\footnote{Data URL withheld for double-blind review; code and dataset link will be released at acceptance.}
An EfficientNet-B3~\cite{tan2019efficientnet} U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} trained on FTW-HD with our augmentation recipe beats a matched S2 baseline -- PRUE~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue} EfficientNet-B7 retrained on the same 25 countries with the \emph{same} augmentation recipe -- on every headline polygon metric (macro over the 11-country held-out split): PQ~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic} 0.324 vs 0.283, mean object F1 over IoU $0.5$--$0.95$ of 0.263 vs 0.225, and mean boundary error 7.4\,m vs 14.8\,m, with a $4\times$ smaller backbone. Pixel IoU favors S2 by 7 points, dragged by two countries (Kenya, Portugal) where the Planet model collapses; on the remaining held-out countries the pixel-IoU gap closes. We argue polygon-level metrics are the right primary measure: downstream pipelines consume vectorized parcels, not rasters. The PQ gain holds across field sizes and 20 of 23 test countries, not only in smallholder regions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Field boundaries feed downstream pipelines for crop-yield estimation, irrigation accounting, subsidy verification, and global food-security monitoring~\cite{beckerreshef2020geoglam,defourny2019sen2agri}. They matter most where they are hardest to see: smallholder farms account for roughly 84\% of the world's farms and produce a disproportionate share of the food consumed in low- and middle-income regions~\cite{lowder2016farms,samberg2016smallholder}. The dominant public benchmark, \emph{Fields of The World} (FTW)~\cite{kerner2024ftw}, packages about 1.6 million parcel polygons across 25 countries paired with 10\,m Sentinel-2 imagery~\cite{drusch2012sentinel2}. Ten meters approaches the resolution floor for smallholder delineation, and several authors~\cite{persello2019delineation,wang2022unlocking,garciapedrero2017boundary} have made this argument directly: a field smaller than $\sim$3 pixels per side cannot be reliably delineated at S2 resolution. \Cref{fig:hero} shows representative FTW-HD patches next to their FTW Sentinel-2 counterparts so the difference is easy to see. Existing PlanetScope benchmarks are either national in scope~\cite{wang2022unlocking,dandrimont2021parcel} or use proprietary labels~\cite{khallaghi2025generalization,estes2022smallholder}, and the global smallholder benchmark AI4SmallFarms~\cite{persello2023ai4smallfarms} stops at 10\,m too.

This paper takes a simple position. We do not need a new benchmark; we need a better image source for the same labels. We pair PlanetScope's 3\,m surface-reflectance imagery~\cite{planetlabs2023psscene} with every FTW patch (the polygon ground truth, the season windows, the country splits) and call the result \textbf{FTW-HD}. We did not re-collect ground truth and we did not retrain the labels; we leveraged the polygons FTW already published and rasterized them onto the PlanetScope grid.

The hard part was getting the imagery. PlanetScope's Orders API is slow and tile-priced; we instead read Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFFs~\cite{cogspec} directly through HTTP range requests, transferring about 1\,MB per patch out of 500\,MB scene strips. The pipeline has three phases (search, activate, range-read) and is fully resumable. Planet's activation API returned broken signed URLs for $\sim$24\% of scenes; a one-shot reactivation pass recovers 99\% of them.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em
    \item A paired 3\,m PlanetScope + UDM2 + 3-class label dataset across all 25 FTW countries: 133{,}168 (patch, window) pairs.
    \item A range-read pipeline that pulls 133k patches off 6{,}113 PlanetScope COGs in $\sim$17\,h wall-time / $\sim$84 CPU-node-h on a commodity AMD EPYC partition, with no Orders API use (\Cref{app:pipeline}).
    \item Per-patch UDM2 quality stats for the full release, plus a re-sampling tool that searches alternative scenes when a patch is locally cloudy.
    \item A baseline recipe (\emph{augmax}) that demonstrates the headroom in 3\,m: an EfficientNet-B3 U-Net trained on FTW-HD beats a PRUE EfficientNet-B7 (S2, full) baseline retrained with the same recipe~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue} on PQ, mean object F1 over IoU $0.5$--$0.95$, and meter-scale boundary error, at $4\times$ smaller backbone.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Field-boundary benchmarks.}
AI4SmallFarms~\cite{persello2023ai4smallfarms} releases 50\,k smallholder parcels across South and Southeast Asia at 10\,m. Single-country datasets exist for France~\cite{wang2022unlocking} and similar~\cite{persello2019delineation}. FTW~\cite{kerner2024ftw} put 25 country-scale releases under one schema and one 3-class mask convention. None of them ship sub-10\,m imagery co-registered with their labels; we change that.

\paragraph{PlanetScope for field segmentation.}
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2022unlocking} use 3\,m PlanetScope for French parcels but go through the Orders API, which clips and serves each patch as an individual product and does not scale. Khallaghi et al.~\cite{khallaghi2025generalization} use Planet Basemaps -- monthly composites, not single scenes -- for cross-year smallholder cropland mapping. Waldner et al.~\cite{waldner2021decode} (DECODE) train field-boundary segmentation on PlanetScope over Australia and report an F-score of $\sim$0.85 on within-distribution test tiles; their codebase targets a single national region and their evaluation does not include polygon-level panoptic or chamfer metrics, so a direct numerical comparison on the FTW 23-country test split is not yet possible on equal terms. We treat DECODE as the closest prior PlanetScope FB method and re-implement its core augmentation hooks where compatible (boundary jitter, multi-scale crop); the head-to-head benchmark on a shared protocol is the natural follow-up that FTW-HD enables. A concurrent line of work releases off-the-shelf field-instance models: \emph{Delineate Anything}~\cite{lavreniuk2025delineate} fine-tunes a YOLO11-seg backbone on a mostly-European RGB mix and ships pretrained weights. It is trained on neither FTW nor PlanetScope SR specifically, so a zero-shot evaluation on FTW-HD is the natural way to compare; we report it in~\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} as one such baseline. We give the community a global, license-clean PlanetScope companion to FTW so that any of these recipes can be re-run at scale.

\paragraph{COG access.}
Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFF~\cite{cogspec} lets a client byte-range-read just the tiles overlapping a region. STAC~\cite{stacspec} exposes the pattern in modern catalogs. PlanetScope's catalog requires an extra step: assets must be \emph{activated} (woken from cold storage) before each scene's signed URL becomes readable, which we describe in~\Cref{app:pipeline}.

\paragraph{Augmentation, instance separation, and metrics.}
Our augmentation recipe layers per-band gamma, affine, Gaussian noise and blur, single-window dropout, small-angle rotation and shear, and boundary jitter on top of the PRUE recipe~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}. CutMix~\cite{yun2019cutmix} did not help us. For instance separation we use marker-controlled watershed~\cite{beucher1993watershed}. For evaluation we report Panoptic Quality~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic} and object F1 averaged over IoU thresholds $0.5$--$0.95$ (in the spirit of COCO's multi-threshold averaging~\cite{lin2014coco}), plus a meter-scale boundary chamfer that is invariant to pixel-grid resolution. We adopt the ``things that helped / things that did not'' ablation-list format from YOLO9000~\cite{redmon2017yolo9000}.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{Source data}

\textbf{FTW patches.} Each FTW v2 country ships $\sim$256$\times$256-pixel chips in EPSG:4326 with two seasonal Sentinel-2 windows per chip and per-patch parquet metadata. We use the chip bounding boxes as the AOI for PlanetScope matching (\Cref{tab:scope}).

\textbf{FTW polygons.} A separate Source Cooperative repository hosts each country's GeoParquet polygons (1.6\,M total)~\cite{ftw-source-coop}. We download these directly and rasterize them onto each PlanetScope patch's grid (\Cref{sec:labels}).

\textbf{PlanetScope.} We target the \texttt{ortho\_analytic\_4b\_sr} asset (4-band Surface Reflectance, $\sim$3\,m, uint16 COG) and \texttt{ortho\_udm2} (8-band quality mask, uint8). The 4-band selection follows the Dove product family; 8-band SuperDove is a drop-in replacement where availability permits.

\subsection{Pairing strategy}

For each FTW chip and each window, we identify a single PSScene whose acquisition date falls inside the window and whose footprint fully covers the chip polygon. We rank candidates first by scene-level cloud cover (Planet's reported \texttt{cloud\_cover}, ceiling $\le$\,10\%), then by absolute day-distance to the window midpoint, with ties broken by item\_id (deterministic). If no candidate covers the chip fully, we drop the (patch, window) target; partial-coverage scenes are not stitched. Patch-level cloud quality is verified \emph{post-hoc} via UDM2 (\Cref{sec:quality}); patches that fail per-band thresholds enter the resampling tool described in that section.

\subsection{Pipeline}
\label{sec:pipeline}

We avoid Planet's Orders API entirely and instead read Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFFs~\cite{cogspec} directly via HTTP range requests through \texttt{rasterio}'s \texttt{/vsicurl/} driver, which transfers $\sim$1\,MB per patch out of $\sim$500\,MB full-scene strips. The pipeline runs three sharded, resumable phases (\emph{search} the Data API, \emph{activate} unique scenes, \emph{extract} all patch windows from each scene at native 3\,m UTM); \Cref{app:pipeline} gives the full description and operational lessons (activation reliability, throughput floors, wall-time breakdown).

\subsection{Labels}
\label{sec:labels}

For each PlanetScope tif we rasterize the country's polygons onto the patch grid as a 3-class mask: 0 (background), 1 (field interior), 2 (boundary). The boundary class is generated by buffering each polygon's exterior ring by 3\,m ($\approx$2 pixels at 3\,m resolution) and rasterizing on top of the interior. This explicitly separates touching parcels, which a binary field/non-field mask collapses. We chose a 3\,m buffer to match the GSD (one Planet pixel either side of the true edge) -- thinner buffers produce sub-pixel rings that the rasterizer drops on diagonal edges, while thicker buffers swallow small interior parcels in smallholder regions.

\paragraph{Patch-level resampling.}
The original search filter rejects scenes whose \emph{scene-level} cloud cover exceeds 10\%; this is a coarse proxy because clouds can concentrate over a single patch even in a low-cloud scene. We provide a re-sampling tool that, for each patch failing any per-band threshold, searches alternative scenes within the same season ($\pm$60\,d), probes their UDM2 over the patch AOI, and replaces the SR + UDM2 pair when a candidate strictly improves quality. The tool re-uses the same activation cache and runs in $\sim$5--8\,h for the full release.

Per-country usable-patch rates vary widely (Portugal 41\%, Netherlands 77\%, vs Brazil/Croatia $>$98\%); full country breakdown in \Cref{app:udm2_country}.

\label{sec:benchmark}

We want a Planet field-boundary model that is at least as good as the
released FTW Sentinel-2 baselines, ideally better, and we want it on a
smaller backbone. This section is a list of things we tried, in order,
on top of the PRUE recipe~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}. Some helped, some
didn't. We report both. \Cref{fig:qualitative} previews the end
result against the released S2 baseline.

\paragraph{How we measure (polygons first).} We lead with four metrics
defined on vectorized parcels rather than rasters: object F1 at
IoU\,$=\,0.5$, panoptic quality~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic} (PQ\,$=$\,SQ$\times$RQ),
object F1 averaged over IoU thresholds $\{0.5, 0.55, \ldots, 0.95\}$~\cite{lin2014coco},
and a mean symmetric boundary chamfer~\cite{cheng2021biou} in meters.
These reward what downstream pipelines actually consume. Pixel IoU is a
noisy proxy: it is dominated by majority background, conflates boundary
localization with bulk-area accuracy, and can be dragged by a single
country where one model collapses. We keep it for continuity with the
released PRUE protocol and make the full case in \Cref{sec:polygon-first}.

\paragraph{Two protocols.} We report on two test sets, and name which
one each number comes from. (1) The FTW \textbf{11-country held-out set}
\cite{kerner2024ftw}: the CC-BY split trains on 14 countries and holds
out 11, matching the released CC-BY PRUE checkpoints. (2) The FTW
\texttt{full\_data} 23-country test split, used when we compare against
the released full-split checkpoints. Our headline numbers (the matched
recipe-vs-recipe comparison in \Cref{tab:polygon_metrics}) are on the
11-country held-out set; the per-country breakdown in
\Cref{fig:per_country_bars} uses the 23-country split. Every metric is
macro-averaged per country following the FTW protocol. Kenya and
Portugal stay in the held-out macro even though both collapse to
$\le 0.10$ pixel IoU under every config we tried; we keep them in every
reported average and discuss their failure modes in
\Cref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{Recipe.} A U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} with
EfficientNet~\cite{tan2019efficientnet} encoder, 8 input channels
(window B then A, 4 bands each), 3-class output,
\texttt{logcoshdice} loss~\cite{milletari2016vnet,jadon2020losses} with class weights $[0.05, 0.20, 0.75]$,
ignore index 3. We train for 100 epochs of AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw} at $10^{-3}$, batch
size 32 (8 for B7), random $512\!\times\!512$ crops, bf16-mixed
precision~\cite{micikevicius2018mixedprec}. We start from this exact recipe and add things until we run
out of ideas. We keep the things that work and we report the things
that didn't.

\subsection{Better augmentations}

    \centering

    \caption{\textbf{Cumulative augmentation lift on Obj F1.} 11-country held-out, WS+TTA inference. FTW-HD recipe progression goes from $26.5$ (PRUE, no extra augs) to $44.6$ (augmax, B3 full data), a $+18.1$\,pp absolute gain.     \label{fig:aug_ablation}
\end{figure}

The biggest lever, by a wide margin (\Cref{fig:aug_ablation}). We
layer four groups in on top of the PRUE recipe; each gets its own
paragraph.

\paragraph{PRUE-style augs we ported over (+$5.4$ pixel IoU on
11-country held-out).} The released PRUE configs enable
\texttt{preprocess\_aug} (random per-batch divisor in
$[5000, 15000]$ replacing the fixed $/10000$) and \texttt{resize\_aug}
(\texttt{RandomResizedCrop} scale $0.3\text{--}0.9$ to the training
crop). Our initial baseline didn't have either. Adding them moves
pixel IoU from $0.444 \rightarrow 0.498$ and Obj F1 (WS) from
$0.265 \rightarrow 0.291$. This single change closes most of the
visible gap to S2 CC-BY at the same B3 backbone.

\paragraph{Window swap and per-band gamma (+$1.9$ pixel IoU, $+1.0$ Obj
F1). \emph{augplus}.} Two cheap additions. \texttt{swap\_order}
randomly flips the A/B window ordering in the 8-channel stack per
sample; the FTW S2 datamodule already supported this and we missed it.
Per-band random gamma ($\gamma \sim \mathcal{U}[0.8, 1.2]$, per channel
independently, $p\!=\!0.3$) models per-band atmospheric/radiometric
variation. We call this configuration \emph{augplus}.

\paragraph{Bespoke aug bundle (+$4.1$ Obj F1 over augplus). \emph{augmax}.}
Seven more aug knobs on top of \emph{augplus}, none individually worth
a paragraph but the bundle helps. Per-band random affine
($y = ax + b$ per channel, $a \sim \mathcal{U}[0.9, 1.1]$,
$b \sim \mathcal{U}[-0.02, 0.02]$, $p\!=\!0.3$); Gaussian blur
($3\!\times\!3$ kernel, $\sigma \sim \mathcal{U}[0.5, 1.5]$,
$p\!=\!0.3$); Gaussian noise ($\sigma\!=\!0.015$, $p\!=\!0.3$);
single-window dropout ($p\!=\!0.15$ per batch to zero out either
window A or window B); small-angle rotation ($\pm 30^\circ$,
$p\!=\!0.5$, applied on top of the $90^\circ$-multiple rotation, H/V
flip, and random sharpness already present in the Kornia~\cite{riba2020kornia} base stack);
shear ($\pm 5^\circ$, $p\!=\!0.3$); and boundary-thickness jitter
(random binary dilation of the boundary class by $0\text{--}2$\,px,
$p\!=\!0.5$ per batch). The \emph{augmax} config additionally keeps
the two earlier baseline-fix augs enabled -- the PRUE
\texttt{preprocess\_aug} (random-divisor input normalization, modeling
cross-acquisition radiometric drift) and \texttt{RandomResizedCrop}
(scale $0.3\text{--}0.9$, ratio $0.75\text{--}1.33$, $p\!=\!0.5$).
Together on 11-country held-out:
$0.300 \rightarrow 0.341$ Obj F1 (WS+TTA),
$0.514 \rightarrow 0.530$ pixel IoU.
The Obj F1 win splits roughly evenly across precision and recall
($+3.4$\,pts each at the watershed F1) -- the model finds more
fields without becoming over-eager. We call this
\emph{augmax}; \emph{augplus} drops the seven bespoke knobs above and
keeps only \texttt{swap\_order} and per-band gamma on top of the
baseline-fix layer.

\subsection{Sharper boundaries}

\paragraph{Watershed post-processing (+$1.0$--$1.3$\,pts Obj F1).}
Marker-controlled watershed with $h$-maxima seeds on a topographic
surface (the predicted SDF when an SDF head is present; otherwise the
Euclidean distance transform of the predicted boundary class).
Consistent gain across every checkpoint. Standard but worth a line.

\paragraph{D4 test-time augmentation (+$0.5$--$1.2$\,pts Obj F1 once
augmentations are on).} D4 8-way flip+rotate ensemble~\cite{wang2019tta,moshkov2020tta}. Was a no-op for
us on the no-aug baselines, which is why we shelved it earlier. Once
the model is regularized by augmax it consistently helps, and it is
cheap to run.

\subsection{Stronger recipe}

\paragraph{Train on all 25 countries (+$6.5$\,pts Obj F1 over CC-BY).}
The released CC-BY checkpoints trained on 14 countries because of
licensing. We retrained on all 25; this lifts our best B3 augmax model
from $0.341 \rightarrow 0.405$ Obj F1 (WS+TTA) on the 11-country
held-out. The CC-BY split is a real licensing constraint but our
strongest single number comes from the full split.

\paragraph{Augmentations $>$ backbone scaling.} At the CC-BY split,
B3 augmax full ($0.405$ Obj F1) beats B7 augmax CC-BY ($0.348$). The
augmentation contribution dwarfs the parameter-count contribution at
this dataset size. Scaling to B7 gives a small lift at the CC-BY
split (\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics}) but \emph{hurts} at the full split
($-2.6$ Obj F1; \Cref{tab:ablation_summary}); B3 is the sweet spot.

\subsection{Polygon metrics as the primary evaluation: full argument}
\label{sec:polygon-first}

Field-boundary models are not consumed as rasters. Downstream
pipelines -- crop-yield estimation, irrigation accounting, subsidy
verification, parcel-level food-security monitoring -- ingest
\emph{vectorized parcels}. What these pipelines need is a polygon that
closes, separates from its neighbors, and lands in the right place
on the ground. We therefore lead with metrics defined on that same
unit: object F1 at IoU\,$=\,0.5$, panoptic
quality~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic} (PQ\,$=$\,SQ$\times$RQ, which
cleanly separates recognition from localization),
object F1 averaged over IoU thresholds $\{0.5,\ldots,0.95\}$~\cite{lin2014coco},
and a mean symmetric boundary chamfer in \emph{meters}.

Polygon-level metrics are also more honest across sensors. Sentinel-2
labels and PlanetScope labels are rasterized at different resolutions from
the same underlying polygon ground truth, so pixel IoU sums two
sources of variation -- bulk-area accuracy and a 1-pixel-wide boundary
class whose physical width differs by $3\times$ -- into a single
scalar. PQ, averaged F1, and meter-scale chamfer are defined on polygons and
are directly comparable: a $3$\,m boundary slip is $3$\,m whether
scored at $3$\,m or $10$\,m.

We retain pixel IoU as a secondary signal for continuity with the
PRUE protocol~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue} and report it in
\Cref{tab:full_data} alongside the polygon metrics that should drive
the comparison.

\paragraph{Pixel IoU is noise-dominated and outlier-dragged.}
Our best Planet model (B3 augmax full, WS+TTA) gets macro pixel IoU
$0.69$ on the 11-country held-out set (\Cref{tab:full_data}); the
S2-augmax B7 full baseline gets $0.76$. The headline $-7$\,pt gap is
dragged by two
countries where the Planet model collapses to near-zero IoU (Kenya:
unlabeled-parcel annotation gaps treated as negatives; Portugal:
sub-pixel terraced micro-fields; see \Cref{sec:limitations}). Removing
those two from the macro closes the gap. We also directly tested
whether evaluation grid drives this number: re-projecting Planet
predictions to the $10$\,m S2 grid changes macro pixel IoU on the
held-out 11 countries by $<0.001$ (\Cref{tab:gsd_controlled},
appendix). The pixel-IoU gap is real on the macro, but it tracks
country-level outliers, not the underlying boundary quality the
metric is meant to summarize. Boundary chamfer in meters, which is
defined on polygons and unaffected by background or outlier
collapses, shows the reverse ($7.4$\,m vs $14.8$\,m;
\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics}).

\paragraph{What we propose instead.} We report four metrics in addition
to obj F1 at IoU=$0.5$: panoptic quality (PQ $=$ SQ$\times$RQ; separates
recognition vs localization), object F1 averaged across IoU thresholds
$\{0.5, 0.55, \ldots, 0.95\}$ (robust to single-threshold brittleness),
polygon-count delta $|N_\text{pred} - N_\text{gt}|$ (over- vs
under-segmentation), and mean symmetric chamfer distance between
predicted and GT boundary pixels in meters
(GSD-invariant -- the same physical $3$\,m boundary slip is $3$\,m
whether we evaluate at $3$\,m or $10$\,m). Table~\ref{tab:polygon_metrics}
reports all four.

The polygon-level picture reverses the pixel-IoU result. FTW-HD B3
\emph{augmax} full beats the strongest S2 baseline on all four
headline polygon metrics: PQ $0.324$ vs $0.283$ ($+4.1$), averaged F1 $0.263$ vs
$0.225$ ($+3.8$), $|\Delta N_\text{poly}|$ $50.9$ vs $60.2$
(closer to ground truth count), and boundary error
$\mathbf{7.4}$\,m vs $\mathbf{14.8}$\,m mean / $22$\,m vs $43$\,m p95
-- predicted boundaries are $\sim\!2\times$ sharper in meters.
S2's only win is SQ ($0.750$ vs $0.710$ for B7 full): matched fields
have marginally tighter per-pixel IoU; but Planet recognizes more
fields and localizes their boundaries more precisely in absolute
distance. PQ, which multiplies recognition and localization, lands
firmly with Planet.

This is the bottom line: \textbf{a B3 PlanetScope model with our
augmax recipe beats a $4\times$-larger PRUE-B7 (S2) full baseline
retrained with the same recipe on all four headline polygon metrics
(PQ, F1$_{[.5:.95]}$, $|\Delta N_\text{poly}|$, boundary chamfer).}

\paragraph{Per-country head-to-head.}
On the FTW \texttt{full\_data} protocol (23-country test split),
macro PQ is $0.379$ (Planet) vs $0.311$ (S2-augmax-B7, same recipe),
a $+6.8$\,pp Planet win. Macro pixel IoU on the 11-country held-out is
$0.69$ vs $0.76$ ($-7$\,pts), dragged by Kenya ($\le\!0.01$) and Portugal ($\le\!0.01$;
both are hard-transfer failures described in \Cref{sec:limitations}).
Figure~\ref{fig:per_country_bars} unpacks PQ into per-country deltas.
Planet wins on $20/23$ countries, ties on one, and loses two
(two-sided sign test over the 22 decided countries:
$p \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-4}$). The
largest gains sit in Northern Europe (netherlands $+19.9$, denmark
$+10.4$, lithuania $+13.0$, luxembourg $+14.7$, brazil $+14.9$) and
extend across most smallholder and mid-latitude landscapes.
The only losses are germany ($-7.2$\,pp) and portugal ($-1.1$\,pp);
estonia is a statistical tie (within $0.01$\,pp).
Germany's loss is consistent with its large regular field geometry,
where S2's boundary class is thick enough to score well even at $10$\,m.

\paragraph{Does the 3\,m GSD advantage track field size?}
Figure~\ref{fig:smallholder_scatter} plots $\Delta$\,PQ against

metrics. Cambodia is now a modest Planet win ($+2.5$\,pp PQ)

\section{Limitations and future work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Coverage.} 7{,}800 (5.5\%) of (patch, window) targets lack a PlanetScope companion in the released index, leaving a net yield of $94.5\%$ (133{,}168 of 140{,}968 targets; \Cref{tab:scope}). The dominant cause is activation failures that never produced a usable URL despite repeated reactivation; a smaller fraction extracted successfully but contained only low-quality imagery (heavy cloud, shadow, or unusable per UDM2) for which the resampling tool found no acceptable alternative scene within the season. These patches still have their original FTW Sentinel-2 imagery but no PlanetScope companion here.

\textbf{Selection bias of dropped patches.} We audited which patches survive QA to test whether the dropped fraction is benign. Per-patch retention is uneven across countries: Portugal (24\%, 66 patches), Finland (75\%), and the Netherlands (77\%) anchor the low end, while Croatia, Brazil, and Latvia exceed 96\%. Dropped patches are systematically cloudier than kept patches (mean max-window cloud cover $0.041$ vs. $0.024$; Mann-Whitney U two-sided $p < 10^{-300}$), with drop rate rising from $13\%$ in the cleanest cloud bin ($<1\%$) to $36\%$ in the cloudiest ($5\text{--}8\%$) within Planet's hard $10\%$ search filter. Conversely, dropped patches have \emph{lower} mean label density than kept patches ($0.276$ vs. $0.366$; $p \approx 10^{-178}$), consistent with the cloudier Nordic and Atlantic countries also being the sparser-labelled ones. We caution that reported per-country performance on Finland, the Netherlands, and Portugal benefits from a cloudier-leaning subset that survived QA, and that the dataset under-represents low-density agricultural scenes in cloudy regions. Full per-country breakdowns and cross-tabs ship alongside the index for transparent reuse.

\textbf{Temporal misalignment.} Our PlanetScope acquisition date is the closest cloud-free match to the FTW season midpoint, not to the exact Sentinel-2 acquisition date. The gap is usually under two weeks but can exceed a month in cloudy regions.

\textbf{Temporal generalization.} FTW-HD ships exactly two seasonal windows per patch, drawn from the FTW v2 season definitions; we make no claim about multi-year or cross-year transfer. Models trained on this release should be re-evaluated before deployment on a different year's imagery.

\textbf{Spectral coverage.} The current release targets the 4-band Dove product (\texttt{ortho\_analytic\_4b\_sr}). 8-band SuperDove imagery would add red-edge and yellow bands that help vegetation discrimination, but FTW v2's labeled windows are historical and most predate SuperDove's full operational coverage, so the 4-band Dove archive is the only product with end-to-end coverage of the FTW season range. A SuperDove companion release is feasible only for the more recent country windows.

\textbf{Hard transfer regions.} Portugal (terraced micro-fields, $<3$\,m wide) and Kenya (FTW's Kenya tile only annotates a subset of fields and leaves surrounding land unlabeled, so our pixelwise loss treats real but unannotated parcels as negatives) collapse to $\le 0.10$ pixel IoU under every configuration we tried. These countries need either targeted data acquisition or a transfer-specific strategy. We do not try to solve them here.

\textbf{Forced monsoon acquisitions (Cambodia).} Cambodia is the only test country forced $100\%$ into monsoon-season PlanetScope acquisitions: the FTW v2 Cambodia window range overlaps only July--August 2021 PSScene coverage, with no dry-season fallback for the resampling tool to draw from. The result is the highest light-haze fraction of any country in the release ($3.7\times$ the global mean) and the lowest \texttt{clear} percentile. The model becomes high-precision / low-recall under that imagery (precision $0.475$, recall $0.294$): it refuses to commit on diffuse boundaries, missing real fields rather than over-segmenting. This is the primary driver of the Cambodia Obj F1 loss; on grid-invariant polygon-quality metrics (PQ, boundary chamfer) Cambodia is in fact a Planet win (see \Cref{sec:polygon-first}). A targeted dry-season re-acquisition is the obvious follow-up.

\textbf{Future work.} We plan to release country-level field-boundary predictions as an annual service for smallholder-dominated countries, where the 3\,m advantage is largest and the operational gap to existing 10\,m products is most consequential. We also plan to investigate resolution-agnostic joint training that combines the Planet and Sentinel-2 versions of FTW in a single model, with the goal of a single checkpoint that ingests either sensor at inference. Both directions are outside the scope of this paper.

\section{Conclusion}

FTW-HD is a 3\,m PlanetScope companion to Fields of The World. We did the engineering work -- range-read the COGs, reactivate the broken URLs, rasterize the labels onto the new grid -- so that others don't have to. We also showed that a small B3 model trained with the right augmentations beats a $4\times$ larger Sentinel-2 baseline trained with the same recipe on all four headline polygon metrics. Field boundaries are where most of the loss happens, and on FTW-HD a 3$\times$ finer grid is now within reach for any FTW user without an Orders API budget.

\section{Access pipeline and operational lessons}
\label{app:pipeline}

The pipeline runs three phases, each independently sharded and resumable from JSONL caches:

\begin{enumerate}
\itemsep0em
    \item \textbf{Search.} Per (patch, window), call the Planet Data API with a date range, geometry filter, and cloud-cover ceiling. Output: best item\_id per patch.
    \item \textbf{Activate.} Deduplicate item\_ids globally; call \texttt{:activate} for SR and UDM2 in parallel. Output: signed Google Cloud Storage URLs.
    \item \textbf{Extract.} Group all (patch, window) by scene, open each COG once with \texttt{rasterio} via \texttt{/vsicurl/}, range-read every member's window, write a local GeoTIFF in PSScene's native UTM at 3\,m. No reprojection.
\end{enumerate}

The activation step dominates wall-time. Cold-storage scenes thaw in 1--46\,min (mean $\sim$200\,s, p99 2{,}763\,s); warm scenes return in 1--5\,s. We saturated Planet's per-account activation queue at $\sim$10\,scenes/min throughput regardless of client concurrency above 32, which sets a floor on time-to-completion.

\paragraph{Activation reliability.} Across 6{,}113 unique scenes, $\sim$24\% of \texttt{:activate} calls returned URLs that subsequently failed with HTTP 400 on the first range read. A second \texttt{:activate} call typically returned a different, working URL; reactivating the failed item\_ids recovered 99\% of these patches. Our final pipeline includes an automatic reactivation pass.

\paragraph{Throughput.} Cold-storage thaw, not bandwidth, is the bottleneck. \Cref{tab:pipeline_cost} reports the wall-time and node-hours for each phase. Total end-to-end build was $\sim$17\,h dominated by activation; pre-warming a list of scene IDs would compress this substantially.

\section{DelineateAnything qualitative comparison}
\label{app:qualitative_delineate}

\Cref{fig:qualitative_delineate} compares our best PRUE-HD-B3 \emph{augmax} full model against the off-the-shelf DelineateAnything~\cite{lavreniuk2025delineate} checkpoint (YOLO11x-seg, RGB uint8 (window B, clip(refl/3000)$\cdot$255), no FTW fine-tuning, canonical inference: conf $=0.005$, NMS IoU $=0.5$, FP16, \texttt{max\_det} raised to $2000$ for dense smallholder patches). We omit their 2500\,m$^{2}$ minimum-area filter so Cambodia/Vietnam smallholders are not excluded by construction. PRUE-HD over-segments in dense parcel grids (watershed seeds at the FB ring fire too eagerly); DelineateAnything systematically returns far fewer instances than there are fields. The qualitative picture matches \Cref{tab:polygon_metrics}: DelineateAnything's SQ rivals our trained models on the masks it does produce, but RQ collapses because it misses most fields.
