\begin{abstract}
Field-boundary benchmarks pair their parcel labels with 10\,m Sentinel-2 imagery, too coarse for the small fields common across much of the world. We introduce \textbf{Fields of the Planet (FTP)}, a 3\,m PlanetScope version of \emph{Fields of The World} (FTW): the same polygon labels and splits, re-paired with sharper imagery.\footnote{Code, release metadata, and dataset access instructions will be released after acceptance.} With the model and training recipe held fixed, moving from 10\,m to 3\,m raises panoptic quality from 0.27 to 0.36 and roughly halves boundary error (17.5 to 7.4\,m), and a small high-resolution model outperforms a much larger one trained at 10\,m. Because field maps are used as polygons, not pixels, we score whole-field recognition and localization rather than pixel overlap, where the gains are largest.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Field boundaries feed downstream pipelines for crop-yield estimation, irrigation accounting, subsidy verification, and global food-security monitoring~\cite{beckerreshef2020geoglam,defourny2019sen2agri}. They matter most where they are hardest to see: smallholder farms account for roughly 84\% of the world's farms and produce a disproportionate share of the food consumed in low- and middle-income regions~\cite{lowder2016farms,samberg2016smallholder}.

The largest public benchmark, \emph{Fields of The World} (FTW)~\cite{kerner2024ftw}, packages about 1.6 million parcel polygons across 24 countries and 25 labeled regions paired with 10\,m Sentinel-2 imagery~\cite{drusch2012sentinel2}. Ten meters approaches the resolution floor for smallholder delineation, and several authors~\cite{persello2019delineation,wang2022unlocking,garciapedrero2017boundary} have made this argument directly: fields on the order of tens of meters per side are difficult to delineate at S2 resolution. Existing high-resolution field-boundary datasets are either geographically narrower, use different label protocols, or are not aligned to FTW splits~\cite{wang2022unlocking,dandrimont2021parcel,estes2024africa,pastishd}.

We pair PlanetScope's 3\,m surface-reflectance imagery~\cite{planetlabs2023psscene} with the FTW polygon ground truth, season windows, and country splits. Each original FTW patch has two seasonal windows, so the 70{,}484 FTW patches become 140{,}968 possible (patch, window) targets; FTP successfully extracts 133{,}168 of them. We rasterize the published FTW polygons onto each PlanetScope grid rather than collecting new labels.

The access pipeline matters because it makes the dataset reproducible at FTW scale. Instead of creating one Orders API product per patch, we activate PlanetScope Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFFs~\cite{cogspec} once and byte-range-read only the tiles overlapping each FTW chip. This reduces data transfer from full-scene strips to about 1\,MB per patch and builds the release in $\sim$17\,h on a CPU partition.

\textbf{This paper makes the following contributions:}
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em
    \item A 3\,m PlanetScope companion to FTW across 24 countries / 25 regions: 133{,}168 image-window pairs with SR imagery and UDM2 masks, plus 123{,}094 3-class label rasters from FTW polygons.
    \item Per-patch UDM2 quality statistics and final QA decisions, so users can filter or audit imagery quality without re-reading the raw Planet assets.
    \item A reproducible COG range-read pipeline that assembles the release from 6{,}113 PlanetScope scenes in $\sim$17\,h / $\sim$84 CPU-node-h without creating per-patch Orders API products (\Cref{app:pipeline}).
    \item A PRUE-compatible benchmark showing that, under matched architectures and training recipes, FTP improves PQ, F1$_{[.5:.95]}$, and meter-scale boundary error over the Sentinel-2 version.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Field-boundary benchmarks.}
AI4SmallFarms~\cite{persello2023ai4smallfarms} releases 50\,k smallholder parcels across South and Southeast Asia at 10\,m. PASTIS and PASTIS-HD provide panoptic agricultural parcel annotations for France with Sentinel-2 time series and aligned 1\,m SPOT 6/7 imagery~\cite{garnot2021pastis,pastishd}. Region- or country-scale high-resolution resources include PlanetScope labels for France and India~\cite{wang2022unlocking}, Africa-wide field-boundary labels digitized on NICFI Planet basemaps~\cite{estes2024africa}, and other national releases~\cite{persello2019delineation,dandrimont2021parcel}. FTW~\cite{kerner2024ftw} is broader geographically and already standardizes semantic, boundary, and instance masks across 24 countries / 25 regions. FTP keeps that label protocol and split structure while adding co-registered 3\,m imagery.

\paragraph{PlanetScope for field segmentation.}
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2022unlocking} use 3\,m PlanetScope for French and Indian parcels. Khallaghi et al.~\cite{khallaghi2025generalization} and Estes et al.~\cite{estes2024africa} use Planet basemap mosaics for smallholder cropland or field-boundary labeling; mosaics improve temporal coverage but can blur scene-level spatial detail. Waldner et al.~\cite{waldner2021decode} (DECODE) train field-boundary segmentation on PlanetScope over Australia, but their data and model weights are not released under a shared FTW-style protocol. \emph{Delineate Anything}~\cite{lavreniuk2025delineate} releases pretrained field-instance models trained on a mostly-European RGB mix. These methods motivate FTP: a shared, global, FTW-aligned high-resolution benchmark where PlanetScope recipes can be compared under one evaluation protocol.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{Source data}

\textbf{FTW patches.} Each FTW region ships geographic chip footprints, two seasonal Sentinel-2 windows per chip, and per-patch parquet metadata. We use the chip footprint as the AOI for PlanetScope matching, not the Sentinel-2 pixel grid (\Cref{tab:scope}). PlanetScope patches are written at native 3\,m resolution, so their pixel dimensions are larger and can vary by footprint; training uses fixed $512\times512$ crops after loading.

\textbf{FTW polygons.} A separate Source Cooperative repository hosts each region's GeoParquet polygons (1.6\,M total)~\cite{ftw-source-coop}. We download these directly and rasterize them onto each PlanetScope patch's grid (\Cref{sec:labels}).

\textbf{PlanetScope.} We target the \texttt{ortho\_analytic\_4b\_sr} asset (4-band Surface Reflectance, $\sim$3\,m, uint16 COG) and \texttt{ortho\_udm2} (8-band quality mask, uint8).

\subsection{Pairing strategy}

For each FTW chip and each seasonal window, we identify a single PSScene whose acquisition date falls inside the window and whose footprint fully covers the chip polygon. We rank candidates first by scene-level cloud cover (Planet's reported \texttt{cloud\_cover}, ceiling $\le$\,10\%), then by absolute day-distance to the window midpoint, with ties broken by item\_id (deterministic). If no candidate covers the chip fully, we drop the (patch, window) target; partial-coverage scenes are not stitched. We use scene-level cloud cover during search because UDM2 is only available after asset activation. After extraction, we compute patch-level UDM2 quality and resample cloudy patches from alternate same-season scenes when a better candidate exists (\Cref{sec:quality}).

\subsection{Pipeline}
\label{sec:pipeline}

We avoid Planet's Orders API entirely and instead read Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFFs~\cite{cogspec} directly via HTTP range requests through \texttt{rasterio}'s \texttt{/vsicurl/} driver, which transfers $\sim$1\,MB per patch out of $\sim$500\,MB full-scene strips. The pipeline runs three sharded, resumable phases (\emph{search} the Data API, \emph{activate} unique scenes, \emph{extract} all patch windows from each scene at native 3\,m UTM); \Cref{app:pipeline} gives the full description and operational lessons (activation reliability, throughput floors, wall-time breakdown).

\subsection{Labels}
\label{sec:labels}

For each PlanetScope tif we rasterize the region's polygons onto the patch grid as a 3-class mask: 0 (background), 1 (field interior), 2 (boundary). The boundary class is generated by buffering each polygon's exterior ring by 3\,m ($\approx$2 pixels at 3\,m resolution) and rasterizing on top of the interior. This follows FTW's semantic/boundary convention and separates touching parcels that a binary field/non-field mask would merge. We use a 3\,m buffer as a one-pixel-per-side GSD heuristic: thinner buffers can disappear on diagonal edges, while thicker buffers can swallow small interiors in dense smallholder regions. We have not ablated the buffer width.

\paragraph{Patch-level resampling.}
The search filter rejects scenes whose \emph{scene-level} cloud cover exceeds 10\%; this is a coarse proxy because clouds can concentrate over a single patch even in a low-cloud scene. The released index uses a patch-level resampling pass: for each patch failing any UDM2 threshold, we search alternative scenes within the same season ($\pm$60\,d), probe UDM2 over the patch AOI, and replace the SR + UDM2 pair when a candidate strictly improves quality. We ship the tool and the final QA table so users can reproduce the decisions or apply stricter thresholds.

Per-country usable-patch rates vary widely (Portugal 41\%, Netherlands 77\%, vs Brazil/Croatia $>$98\%); full country breakdown in \Cref{app:udm2_country}.

\label{sec:benchmark}

We use PRUE~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue} as the benchmark recipe because it is the most directly comparable published FTW training setup. The main question is whether the imagery change itself helps: if we keep the architecture, loss, training schedule, and post-processing comparable, does replacing Sentinel-2 with FTP improve field-boundary polygons? We answer that with matched B3 and B7 U-Net baselines, then report the augmentation and post-processing changes needed to make the PlanetScope baseline competitive. \Cref{fig:qualitative} previews the final PlanetScope model against the S2 baseline.

\paragraph{How we measure (polygons first).} We lead with four metrics
defined on vectorized parcels rather than rasters: object F1 at
IoU\,$=\,0.5$, panoptic quality~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic} (PQ\,$=$\,SQ$\times$RQ),
object F1 averaged over IoU thresholds $\{0.5, 0.55, \ldots, 0.95\}$~\cite{lin2014coco},
and a mean symmetric boundary chamfer~\cite{cheng2021biou} in meters.
These reward what downstream pipelines consume. Pixel IoU is secondary:
it is dominated by majority background and conflates boundary localization
with bulk-area accuracy. We keep pixel IoU only for continuity
with the released PRUE protocol and make the full case in
\Cref{sec:polygon-first}.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocols.} We separate two comparisons. The
\textbf{CC-BY split} trains on 14 countries and evaluates country transfer
on the 11 held-out FTW countries, matching the released CC-BY PRUE
checkpoints. The \textbf{full-data split} follows the FTW full-data
protocol: models train on the full 24-country / 25-region release and are
evaluated on held-out patches from the test regions. Our architecture-matched
dataset comparison uses the same training split on both sensors. Kenya's FTW
labels are presence-only (background untrusted), so its supervised pixel and
object metrics are not comparable: all held-out macro-averages in this paper
are over the 10 dense-label countries, and Kenya appears only as a clearly
marked presence-only row (\Cref{tab:heldout_pc}, \Cref{sec:limitations}).
Portugal remains in all metrics and is discussed as a hard transfer case in
\Cref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{PRUE-compatible recipe.} Unless otherwise stated, each model is
a U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} with an EfficientNet encoder~\cite{tan2019efficientnet},
8 input channels (window B then A; 4 bands per window), and 3 output
classes. We train with \texttt{logcoshdice} loss~\cite{milletari2016vnet,jadon2020losses},
class weights $[0.05,0.20,0.75]$, ignore index 3, AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw}
at $10^{-3}$, 100 epochs, batch size 32 for B3 / 8 for B7, random
$512\!\times\!512$ crops, and bf16-mixed precision~\cite{micikevicius2018mixedprec}.
This gives a controlled S2-vs-Planet comparison before we add Planet-specific
regularization.

\subsection{Main result}

\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} isolates the dataset effect under the same
architecture and training recipe. With an EfficientNet-B3 backbone, replacing
Sentinel-2 with FTP improves PQ from 0.271 to 0.360, F1$_{[.5:.95]}$
from 0.210 to 0.291, and mean boundary error from 17.5\,m to 7.4\,m. FTP
B3 also outperforms the larger S2 EfficientNet-B7 baseline trained with the
same augmax recipe on PQ (0.360 vs 0.311), F1$_{[.5:.95]}$ (0.291 vs 0.247),
and boundary error (7.4\,m vs 15.2\,m); the S2 B7 model retains a slight edge
on polygon-count error ($|\Delta N|$ 54.9 vs 55.5). These comparisons use
object and polygon metrics because both sensors are scored after
vectorization from the same FTW polygon source.

\subsection{Better augmentations}

\begin{figure}[t!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figs/aug_ablation_paper.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Cumulative augmentation lift on Obj F1.} Dense-label held-out evaluation (10 countries) with WS+TTA inference. The Planet B3 recipe improves from 0.291 (PRUE recipe, no extra augmentations) to 0.452 (augmax, full-data training). Dotted horizontal lines mark S2 baselines trained with the same augmax recipe; the architecture-matched B3 comparison isolates the dataset effect.}
    \label{fig:aug_ablation}
\end{figure}

Data augmentations were the largest lever for PlanetScope performance
(\Cref{fig:aug_ablation}). We group the sweep into three stages so the
comparison is easy to audit: PRUE normalization/crop augmentations,
temporal/radiometric augmentations, and a final geometry/noise bundle.

\paragraph{PRUE normalization and crop augmentations.} The released PRUE configs enable
\texttt{preprocess\_aug} (random per-batch divisor in
$[5000, 15000]$ replacing the fixed $/10000$) and \texttt{resize\_aug}
(\texttt{RandomResizedCrop} scale $0.3\text{--}0.9$ to the training
crop). Adding them moves Obj F1 (WS) from $0.291 \rightarrow 0.319$.

\paragraph{Window swap and per-band gamma (+$1.0$ Obj
F1). \emph{augplus}.} Two cheap additions. \texttt{swap\_order}
randomly flips the A/B window ordering in the 8-channel stack per
sample; the FTW S2 datamodule already supported this and we missed it.
Per-band random gamma ($\gamma \sim \mathcal{U}[0.8, 1.2]$, per channel
independently, $p\!=\!0.3$) models per-band atmospheric/radiometric
variation. We call this configuration \emph{augplus}.

\paragraph{Geometry/noise bundle (+$4.1$ Obj F1 over augplus). \emph{augmax}.}
Seven augmentations on top of \emph{augplus} form the final bundle. Per-band random affine
($y = ax + b$ per channel, $a \sim \mathcal{U}[0.9, 1.1]$,
$b \sim \mathcal{U}[-0.02, 0.02]$, $p\!=\!0.3$); Gaussian blur
($3\!\times\!3$ kernel, $\sigma \sim \mathcal{U}[0.5, 1.5]$,
$p\!=\!0.3$); Gaussian noise ($\sigma\!=\!0.015$, $p\!=\!0.3$);
single-window dropout ($p\!=\!0.15$ per batch to zero out either
window A or window B); small-angle rotation ($\pm 30^\circ$,
$p\!=\!0.5$, applied on top of the $90^\circ$-multiple rotation, H/V
flip, and random sharpness already present in the Kornia~\cite{riba2020kornia} base stack);
shear ($\pm 5^\circ$, $p\!=\!0.3$); and boundary-thickness jitter
(random binary dilation of the boundary class by $0\text{--}2$\,px,
$p\!=\!0.5$ per batch). The \emph{augmax} config additionally keeps
the two earlier baseline-fix augs enabled -- the PRUE
\texttt{preprocess\_aug} (random-divisor input normalization, modeling
cross-acquisition radiometric drift) and \texttt{RandomResizedCrop}
(scale $0.3\text{--}0.9$, ratio $0.75\text{--}1.33$, $p\!=\!0.5$).
Together on the 10-country dense held-out:
$0.329 \rightarrow 0.374$ Obj F1 (WS+TTA),
$0.564 \rightarrow 0.581$ pixel IoU.
The Obj F1 gain splits roughly evenly across precision and recall
($+3.4$\,pts each at the watershed F1). We call this \emph{augmax};
\emph{augplus} drops the seven bundle augmentations and keeps only
\texttt{swap\_order} and per-band gamma on top of the PRUE
normalization/crop stage.

\subsection{Sharper boundaries}

\paragraph{Watershed post-processing (+$0.4$--$0.8$\,pts Obj F1).}
Marker-controlled watershed with $h$-maxima seeds on a topographic
surface (the predicted SDF when an SDF head is present; otherwise the
Euclidean distance transform of the predicted boundary class) gives a
consistent gain across checkpoints.

\paragraph{D4 test-time augmentation (+$0.5$--$1.1$\,pts Obj F1 once
augmentations are on).} We use an 8-way flip+rotate ensemble~\cite{wang2019tta,moshkov2020tta}.
D4 TTA has little effect on the no-augmentation baseline but improves
models trained with \emph{augmax}.

\subsection{Stronger recipe}

\paragraph{Train on all 24 countries / 25 regions (+$7.8$\,pts Obj F1 over CC-BY).}
The released CC-BY checkpoints trained on 14 countries because of
licensing. We retrained on the full release; this lifts the B3 augmax model
from $0.374 \rightarrow 0.452$ Obj F1 (WS+TTA) on the 10-country dense
evaluation set. This is not a country-transfer claim; it measures the
full-data protocol used by the released full PRUE checkpoints.

\paragraph{Augmentations matter more than backbone scaling here.}
Scaling B3 to B7 gives a small lift on the CC-BY split
(\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics}) but hurts on the full-data split
($-1.6$ Obj F1; \Cref{tab:ablation_summary}). We therefore use B3 as
the main PlanetScope baseline.

\subsection{Metric choice}
\label{sec:polygon-first}

Field-boundary models are not consumed as rasters. Downstream
pipelines -- crop-yield estimation, irrigation accounting, subsidy
verification, parcel-level food-security monitoring -- ingest
\emph{vectorized parcels}. What these pipelines need is a polygon that
closes, separates from its neighbors, and lands in the right place
on the ground. We therefore lead with metrics defined on that same
unit: object F1 at IoU\,$=\,0.5$, panoptic
quality~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic} (PQ\,$=$\,SQ$\times$RQ, which
cleanly separates recognition from localization),
object F1 averaged over IoU thresholds $\{0.5,\ldots,0.95\}$~\cite{lin2014coco},
and a mean symmetric boundary chamfer in \emph{meters}.

Polygon-level metrics are less tied to raster-grid resolution. Sentinel-2
labels and PlanetScope labels are rasterized at different resolutions from
the same underlying polygon ground truth, so pixel IoU combines two
sources of variation -- bulk-area accuracy and a 1-pixel-wide boundary
class whose physical width differs by $3\times$ -- into a single
scalar. PQ, averaged F1, and meter-scale chamfer are defined on polygons and
are directly comparable: a $3$\,m boundary slip is $3$\,m whether
scored at $3$\,m or $10$\,m.

We retain pixel IoU as a secondary signal for continuity with the
PRUE protocol~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}. We do not use it as a headline
metric, and we do not interpret Kenya pixel IoU because the Kenya labels
are presence-only.

\paragraph{Pixel IoU is secondary.}
Pixel IoU remains useful for reproducing the PRUE protocol, but it is a
poor summary of field-boundary utility here. The metric is sensitive to
majority background, the physical width of the rasterized boundary class,
and country-specific annotation gaps. In Kenya, FTW labels are
presence-only, so treating unlabeled parcels as negatives makes pixel IoU
misleading. We therefore report pixel IoU only in the protocol-comparison
table and use polygon metrics for the main claims. A controlled
re-gridding check shows that re-projecting Planet predictions to the
$10$\,m S2 grid changes macro pixel IoU by $<0.001$
(\Cref{tab:gsd_controlled}, appendix).

\paragraph{Object metrics.} We report four metrics in addition
to object F1 at IoU=$0.5$: panoptic quality (PQ $=$ SQ$\times$RQ; separates
recognition vs localization), object F1 averaged across IoU thresholds
$\{0.5, 0.55, \ldots, 0.95\}$ (robust to single-threshold brittleness),
polygon-count delta $|N_\text{pred} - N_\text{gt}|$ (over- vs
under-segmentation), and mean symmetric chamfer distance between
predicted and GT boundary pixels in meters
(GSD-invariant -- the same physical $3$\,m boundary slip is $3$\,m
whether we evaluate at $3$\,m or $10$\,m). Table~\ref{tab:polygon_metrics}
reports all four.

\paragraph{Per-region head-to-head.}
On the FTW \texttt{full\_data} protocol, macro PQ across the 23 regions
with complete polygon-metric outputs is $0.383$ (Planet) vs $0.311$
(S2-augmax-B7, same recipe), a $+7.2$\,pp Planet win. Figure~\ref{fig:per_country_bars}
unpacks PQ into per-region deltas. Planet wins on $21/23$ regions and
loses two (two-sided sign test over the 23 decided regions:
$p \approx 6.6\times10^{-5}$).
The largest gains include Netherlands $+20.1$, Brazil $+15.5$,
Luxembourg $+15.3$, Lithuania $+13.7$, and Corsica $+10.6$.
The losses are Germany ($-7.0$\,pp) and Portugal ($-0.2$\,pp).
Germany's loss is consistent with its large regular field geometry,
where S2's boundary class is thick enough to score well even at $10$\,m.

\paragraph{Does the 3\,m GSD advantage track field size?}
Figure~\ref{fig:smallholder_scatter} plots $\Delta$\,PQ against
per-region median FTW field area. The trend is weak (Pearson $r$ $=$
$+0.10$ on log-hectares, Spearman $\rho$ $=$ $+0.11$, $n$ $=$ 22):
the PQ gain is spread across regions and not tied to parcel size alone.
Small-field regions do anchor several top wins (Luxembourg $+14.7$\,pp at
$0.3$\,ha; Rwanda $+9.7$\,pp at $1.4$\,ha; Lithuania $+13.0$\,pp at
$1.4$\,ha), but so does large-field Netherlands
($+19.9$\,pp), whose PQ gain comes from sharper instance separation
rather than resolving sub-pixel parcels.
Germany is the one country that consistently favors S2 ($-7.2$\,pp);
its large rectangular fields give S2's coarser boundary ring
enough pixel coverage to score competitively on detection-heavy
metrics. Cambodia is now a modest Planet win ($+2.5$\,pp PQ)

(see \Cref{sec:limitations}).

\section{Limitations and future work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Coverage.} 7{,}800 (5.5\%) of (patch, window) targets lack a PlanetScope companion in the released index, leaving a net yield of $94.5\%$ (133{,}168 of 140{,}968 targets; \Cref{tab:scope}). The dominant cause is activation failures that never produced a usable URL despite repeated reactivation; a smaller fraction extracted successfully but contained only low-quality imagery (heavy cloud, shadow, or unusable per UDM2) for which the resampling tool found no acceptable alternative scene within the season. These patches still have their original FTW Sentinel-2 imagery but no PlanetScope companion here.

\textbf{Selection bias of dropped patches.} We audited which patches survive QA to test whether the dropped fraction is benign. Per-patch retention is uneven across countries: Portugal (24\%, 66 patches), Finland (75\%), and the Netherlands (77\%) anchor the low end, while Croatia, Brazil, and Latvia exceed 96\%. Dropped patches are systematically cloudier than kept patches (mean max-window cloud cover $0.041$ vs. $0.024$; Mann-Whitney U two-sided $p < 10^{-300}$), with drop rate rising from $13\%$ in the cleanest cloud bin ($<1\%$) to $36\%$ in the cloudiest ($5\text{--}8\%$) within Planet's hard $10\%$ search filter. Conversely, dropped patches have \emph{lower} mean label density than kept patches ($0.276$ vs. $0.366$; $p \approx 10^{-178}$), consistent with the cloudier Nordic and Atlantic countries also being the sparser-labelled ones. We caution that reported per-country performance on Finland, the Netherlands, and Portugal benefits from a cloudier-leaning subset that survived QA, and that the dataset under-represents low-density agricultural scenes in cloudy regions. Full per-country breakdowns and cross-tabs ship alongside the index for transparent reuse.

\textbf{Temporal misalignment.} Our PlanetScope acquisition date is the closest cloud-free match to the FTW season midpoint, not to the exact Sentinel-2 acquisition date. The gap is usually under two weeks but can exceed a month in cloudy regions.

\textbf{Temporal generalization.} FTP ships exactly two seasonal windows per patch, drawn from the FTW v2 season definitions; we make no claim about multi-year or cross-year transfer. Models trained on this release should be re-evaluated before deployment on a different year's imagery.

\textbf{Spectral coverage.} The current release targets the 4-band Dove product (\texttt{ortho\_analytic\_4b\_sr}). 8-band SuperDove imagery would add red-edge and yellow bands that help vegetation discrimination, but FTW v2's labeled windows are historical and most predate SuperDove's full operational coverage, so the 4-band Dove archive is the only product with end-to-end coverage of the FTW season range. A SuperDove companion release is feasible only for the more recent country windows.

\textbf{Presence-only and hard transfer regions.} Kenya is presence-only in FTW: the tile annotates a subset of fields and leaves surrounding land unlabeled, so its background class is untrusted and supervised pixel/object metrics computed against it are not comparable across models. We therefore exclude Kenya from every supervised macro-average in this paper and report it only as a clearly marked presence-only row (\Cref{tab:heldout_pc}). Our evaluation tooling supports masking presence-only backgrounds to the ignore index at evaluation time, but the training labels still rasterize unlabeled Kenya land as background; a cleaner comparison should retrain with presence-only pixels masked out. Portugal remains a hard transfer case because its terraced micro-fields are near or below the PlanetScope GSD. These regions need either targeted data acquisition or a transfer-specific strategy.

\textbf{Forced monsoon acquisitions (Cambodia).} Cambodia is forced into monsoon-season PlanetScope acquisitions: the FTW v2 Cambodia window range overlaps only July--August 2021 PSScene coverage, with no dry-season fallback for the resampling tool to draw from. The result is the highest light-haze fraction of any region in the release ($3.7\times$ the global mean) and the lowest \texttt{clear} percentile. The model becomes high-precision / low-recall under that imagery (precision $0.475$, recall $0.294$), missing fields with diffuse boundaries. This is the primary driver of the Cambodia Obj F1 loss; on PQ and boundary chamfer Cambodia is still a Planet win (see \Cref{sec:polygon-first}).

\textbf{Future work.} Country-scale inference is the next practical test of FTP, especially in regions where small fields and dense parcel boundaries make 10\,m products brittle. We also plan to release trained PlanetScope checkpoints and investigate resolution-agnostic joint training that combines the Planet and Sentinel-2 versions of FTW in a single model. These directions are outside the scope of this paper.

\section{Conclusion}

Fields of the Planet (FTP) is a 3\,m PlanetScope companion to Fields of The World. It releases co-registered PlanetScope imagery, UDM2 quality masks, rasterized FTW labels, and the range-read pipeline used to build them. Under a matched PRUE recipe, FTP improves polygon-level field delineation over Sentinel-2 on PQ, F1$_{[.5:.95]}$, polygon-count error, and meter-scale boundary error. The result is a high-resolution FTW benchmark that keeps the original label protocol and splits while reducing the operational cost of working with PlanetScope imagery.

\section{Access pipeline and operational lessons}
\label{app:pipeline}

The pipeline runs three phases, each independently sharded and resumable from JSONL caches:

\begin{enumerate}
\itemsep0em
    \item \textbf{Search.} Per (patch, window), call the Planet Data API with a date range, geometry filter, and cloud-cover ceiling. Output: best item\_id per patch.
    \item \textbf{Activate.} Deduplicate item\_ids globally; call \texttt{:activate} for SR and UDM2 in parallel. Output: signed Google Cloud Storage URLs.
    \item \textbf{Extract.} Group all (patch, window) by scene, open each COG once with \texttt{rasterio} via \texttt{/vsicurl/}, range-read every member's window, write a local GeoTIFF in PSScene's native UTM at 3\,m. No reprojection.
\end{enumerate}

The activation step dominates wall-time. Cold-storage scenes thaw in 1--46\,min (mean $\sim$200\,s, p99 2{,}763\,s); warm scenes return in 1--5\,s. We saturated Planet's per-account activation queue at $\sim$10\,scenes/min throughput regardless of client concurrency above 32, which sets a floor on time-to-completion.

\paragraph{Activation reliability.} Across 6{,}113 unique scenes, $\sim$24\% of \texttt{:activate} calls returned URLs that subsequently failed with HTTP 400 on the first range read. A second \texttt{:activate} call typically returned a different, working URL; reactivating the failed item\_ids recovered 99\% of these patches. Our final pipeline includes an automatic reactivation pass.

\paragraph{Throughput.} Cold-storage thaw, not bandwidth, is the bottleneck. \Cref{tab:pipeline_cost} reports the wall-time and node-hours for each phase. Total end-to-end build was $\sim$17\,h dominated by activation; pre-warming a list of scene IDs would compress this substantially.
