\begin{abstract}
Fields of The World (FTW) pairs global field-boundary labels with publicly available 10m Sentinel-2 imagery, making global agricultural field delineation practical at scale. But in many regions smallholder fields are too small to resolve accurately at Sentinel-2's 10m resolution. We introduce \textbf{Fields of the Planet (FTP)}, a 3m PlanetScope variant of FTW that keeps the same polygon labels and data splits but pairs them with higher-resolution imagery. With the task, geography, labels, architecture, and training recipe all fixed, training on PlanetScope instead of Sentinel-2 improves panoptic quality from 27.1 to 36.0 (on a 0--100 scale) and more than halves mean boundary error, from 17.5m to 7.4m. We additionally evaluate with polygon-level metrics that score whether predicted fields are recognized and localized as usable geometric parcels, rather than measuring overlap at the pixel level. \footnote{Code, release metadata, and dataset access instructions will be released after acceptance.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Field boundaries feed downstream pipelines for crop-yield estimation, irrigation accounting, subsidy verification, and global food-security monitoring~\cite{beckerreshef2020geoglam,defourny2019sen2agri}. They matter most where they are hardest to see: farms under 2\,ha account for roughly 84\% of the world's farms \emph{by count}~\cite{lowder2016farms,samberg2016smallholder}, and these smallholdings produce a disproportionate share of the food consumed in low- and middle-income regions. Their parcels are also the smallest --- often only tens of meters across --- so the imagery resolution that maps them is exactly where a field boundary either resolves or disappears. This tension between \emph{where boundaries matter most} and \emph{where they are hardest to resolve} motivates the two choices in this paper: the move to higher-resolution imagery, and the polygon-level metrics we adopt to test whether that move actually recovers small fields as usable geometry.

The largest public benchmark, \emph{Fields of The World} (FTW)~\cite{kerner2024ftw}, packages 1{,}627{,}378 parcel polygons across 24 countries and 25 labeled regions paired with 10m Sentinel-2 imagery~\cite{drusch2012sentinel2}. Ten meters approaches the resolution floor for smallholder delineation, and several authors~\cite{persello2019delineation,wang2022unlocking,garciapedrero2017boundary} have made this argument directly: fields on the order of tens of meters per side are difficult to delineate at S2 resolution --- a sub-hectare parcel can span only a handful of 10m pixels, leaving its boundary indistinguishable from interior texture. Existing high-resolution field-boundary datasets are either geographically narrower, use different label protocols, or are not aligned to FTW splits~\cite{wang2022unlocking,dandrimont2021parcel,estes2024africa,pastishd}.

PlanetScope is Planet Labs' constellation of $\sim$130 Dove CubeSats that images the entire land surface near-daily at $\sim$3m ground sample distance~\cite{planetlabs2023psscene} --- roughly $3\times$ finer than Sentinel-2, and enough to place several pixels across the smallholder parcels that S2 cannot resolve. We pair PlanetScope's 3m surface-reflectance imagery with the FTW polygon ground truth, season windows, and country splits, and rasterize the published FTW polygons onto each PlanetScope grid rather than collecting new labels. Each FTW patch has two seasonal windows, so the 70{,}484 FTW patches define 140{,}968 (patch, window) targets; FTP pairs 133{,}168 of them (94.5\%) with a cloud-screened PlanetScope acquisition, leaving 7{,}800 targets without a usable scene (\Cref{sec:limitations}).

\textbf{This paper makes the following contributions:}
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em
    \item A 3m PlanetScope companion to FTW across 24 countries / 25 regions: 66{,}584 patches, each pairing a planting- and harvest-season image (133{,}168 SR images and UDM2 masks) with a corresponding 3-class label mask rasterized from the FTW polygons.
    \item Per-patch UDM2 quality statistics and final QA decisions, so users can filter or audit imagery quality without re-reading the raw Planet assets.
    \item A reproducible, sharded access pipeline that assembles the release from 6{,}113 PlanetScope scenes (\Cref{app:pipeline}).
    \item A benchmark built on the released FTW training recipe (PRUE~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}) showing that, under matched architectures and training recipes, FTP improves PQ, F1$_{[.5:.95]}$, and meter-scale boundary error over the Sentinel-2 version.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Field-boundary benchmarks.}
AI4SmallFarms~\cite{persello2023ai4smallfarms} releases 50\,k smallholder parcels across South and Southeast Asia at 10m. PASTIS and PASTIS-HD provide panoptic agricultural parcel annotations for France with Sentinel-2 time series and aligned 1m SPOT 6/7 imagery~\cite{garnot2021pastis,pastishd}. Region- or country-scale high-resolution resources include PlanetScope labels for France and India~\cite{wang2022unlocking}, Africa-wide field-boundary labels digitized on NICFI Planet basemaps~\cite{estes2024africa}, and other national releases~\cite{persello2019delineation,dandrimont2021parcel}. FTW~\cite{kerner2024ftw} is broader geographically and already standardizes semantic, boundary, and instance masks across 24 countries / 25 regions. FTP keeps that label protocol and split structure while adding co-registered 3m imagery.

\paragraph{PlanetScope for field segmentation.}
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2022unlocking} use 3m PlanetScope for French and Indian parcels. Khallaghi et al.~\cite{khallaghi2025generalization} and Estes et al.~\cite{estes2024africa} use Planet basemap mosaics for smallholder cropland or field-boundary labeling; mosaics improve temporal coverage but can blur scene-level spatial detail. Waldner et al.~\cite{waldner2021decode} (DECODE) train field-boundary segmentation on PlanetScope over Australia, but their data and model weights are not released under a shared FTW-style protocol. \emph{Delineate Anything}~\cite{lavreniuk2025delineate} releases pretrained field-instance models trained on a mostly-European RGB mix. These methods motivate FTP: a shared, global, FTW-aligned high-resolution benchmark where PlanetScope recipes can be compared under one evaluation protocol.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{Source data}

\textbf{FTW patches.} Each FTW region ships geographic chip footprints, two seasonal Sentinel-2 windows per chip, and per-patch parquet metadata. We use the chip footprint as the AOI for PlanetScope matching, not the Sentinel-2 pixel grid (\Cref{tab:scope}). Each patch is written at native 3m resolution, so its pixel dimensions vary with the chip footprint (typically $\sim$510$\times$330\,px) rather than matching the fixed 256$\times$256 S2 grid; for training we pad with the ignore label and take fixed $512\times512$ crops (\Cref{sec:benchmark}).

\textbf{FTW polygons.} A separate Source Cooperative repository hosts each region's GeoParquet polygons (1{,}627{,}378 total)~\cite{ftw-source-coop}. We download these directly and rasterize them onto each PlanetScope patch's grid (\Cref{sec:labels}).

\textbf{PlanetScope.} We target the \texttt{ortho\_analytic\_4b\_sr} asset (4-band Surface Reflectance, $\sim$3m, uint16 COG) and \texttt{ortho\_udm2} (8-band quality mask, uint8).

\subsection{Pairing strategy}

For each FTW chip and each seasonal window, we identify a single PSScene whose acquisition date falls inside the window and whose footprint fully covers the chip polygon. We rank candidates first by scene-level cloud cover (Planet's reported \texttt{cloud\_cover}, ceiling $\le$\,10\%), then by absolute day-distance to the window midpoint, with ties broken by item\_id (deterministic). If no candidate covers the chip fully, we drop the (patch, window) target; partial-coverage scenes are not stitched.

We use scene-level cloud cover during search because UDM2 is only available after asset activation. After extraction, we compute patch-level UDM2 quality and resample cloudy patches from alternate same-season scenes when a better candidate exists (\Cref{sec:quality}).

\subsection{Labels}
\label{sec:labels}

For each PlanetScope tif we rasterize the region's polygons onto the patch grid as a 3-class mask: 0 (background), 1 (field interior), 2 (boundary). The boundary class is generated by buffering each polygon's exterior ring by 3m ($\approx$2 pixels at 3m resolution) and rasterizing on top of the interior. This follows FTW's semantic/boundary convention and separates touching parcels that a binary field/non-field mask would merge. We use a 3m buffer as a one-pixel-per-side GSD heuristic: thinner buffers can disappear on diagonal edges, while thicker buffers can swallow small interiors in dense smallholder regions. We have not ablated the buffer width.

\section{Per-patch quality analysis}
\label{sec:quality}

We compute per-patch UDM2 statistics across the full 133\,k UDM2 release by reading the patch-area windows directly from each mask and counting positive pixels per band. \textbf{86.6\%} of patches are usable at a strict threshold (clear$\,\ge\,$95\%, unusable$\,\le\,$5\%); the per-band percentile breakdown is in \Cref{tab:udm2} (appendix).

\paragraph{Patch-level resampling.}
The search filter rejects scenes whose \emph{scene-level} cloud cover exceeds 10\%; this is a coarse proxy because clouds can concentrate over a single patch even in a low-cloud scene. The released index uses a patch-level resampling pass: for each patch failing any UDM2 threshold, we search alternative scenes within the same season ($\pm$60\,d), probe UDM2 over the patch AOI, and replace the SR + UDM2 pair when a candidate strictly improves quality. We ship the tool and the final QA table so users can reproduce the decisions or apply stricter thresholds.

Per-country usable-patch rates vary widely (Portugal 41\%, Netherlands 77\%, vs Brazil/Croatia $>$98\%); full country breakdown in \Cref{app:udm2_country}.

We use PRUE~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue} as the benchmark recipe because it is the most directly comparable published FTW training setup. The main question is whether the imagery change itself helps: if we keep the architecture, loss, training schedule, and post-processing comparable, does replacing Sentinel-2 with FTP improve field-boundary polygons? We answer that with matched EfficientNet-B3 and EfficientNet-B7 U-Net baselines, then report the augmentation and post-processing changes needed to make the PlanetScope baseline competitive. \Cref{fig:qualitative} previews the final PlanetScope model against the S2 baseline.

\paragraph{How we measure (polygons first).} A pixel-overlap score can
look healthy while every small field in a patch is merged into its
neighbours --- exactly the failure that higher resolution is meant to
fix. We therefore lead with four metrics defined on \emph{vectorized
parcels}, all computed on the field-interior class, so that ``did we
recover this small field as a usable polygon?'' is the question being
scored (\Cref{fig:metric_example}). We report four rather than one
because parcel quality fails in distinct ways --- a field can go
undetected, be detected but poorly shaped, or be well-shaped yet
mislocated at its edges --- and any single scalar hides which failure
occurred:
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em
  \item \textbf{Object F1 at IoU\,$=\,0.5$} --- \emph{did we find the right
  fields?} A predicted parcel counts as a true positive when it overlaps a
  ground-truth parcel with IoU\,$\ge\,0.5$; F1 is the harmonic mean of the
  resulting precision (of the parcels we predict, how many are real) and
  recall (of the real parcels, how many we find).
  \item \textbf{Panoptic quality}~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic}
  (PQ\,$=$\,SQ$\times$RQ) --- \emph{did we find the fields, and are the ones
  we found the right shape?} Recognition quality RQ is the object F1 above
  (detection); segmentation quality SQ is the mean IoU of the matched parcels
  (how tightly each found parcel sits on the true one). Their product
  separates ``found the field'' from ``placed it accurately''.
  \item \textbf{Object F1 averaged over IoU thresholds}
  $\{0.5, 0.55, \ldots, 0.95\}$~\cite{lin2014coco} --- \emph{did we find them
  precisely, not just roughly?} Raising the required overlap from 0.5 toward
  0.95 only credits increasingly exact parcels, rewarding tight localization
  over loose detection.
  \item \textbf{Mean symmetric boundary chamfer}~\cite{cheng2021biou}, in
  meters --- \emph{are the field edges in the right place?} The average
  distance from a predicted parcel boundary to the nearest ground-truth
  boundary, symmetrized with the reverse direction, in physical units --- so
  a $3$\,m edge slip reads as $3$\,m regardless of pixel size.
\end{itemize}
Formal, implementation-exact definitions of every metric are given in
\Cref{app:metrics}. These reward what downstream pipelines consume. Pixel IoU is secondary:
it is dominated by majority background and conflates boundary localization
with bulk-area accuracy. We keep pixel IoU only for continuity
with the released PRUE protocol and make the full case in
\Cref{sec:polygon-first}.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocols.} We separate two comparisons. The
\textbf{CC-BY split} mirrors the released CC-BY PRUE checkpoints: it trains
on the 14 CC-BY--licensed FTW countries and evaluates on the remaining 11
held-out countries, so those countries are never seen during training. The
\textbf{full-data split} follows the FTW full-data protocol: models train on
the full 24-country / 25-region release and are evaluated on held-out patches
from the test regions. Our architecture-matched dataset comparison uses the
same training split on both sensors. Independent of the split, Kenya is the
\emph{only} presence-only region in FTW v2: it annotates a subset of fields
and leaves surrounding land unlabeled, so its background class is untrusted
and supervised pixel/object metrics computed against it are not comparable
across models. We therefore report all held-out macro-averages over the 10
dense-label countries, and Kenya appears only as a clearly marked
presence-only row (\Cref{tab:heldout_pc}, \Cref{sec:limitations}). Portugal
has trusted labels but remains a hard transfer case (terraced micro-fields
near the PlanetScope GSD); it stays in all metrics and is discussed in
\Cref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{PRUE-compatible recipe.} Unless otherwise stated, each model is
a U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} with an EfficientNet encoder~\cite{tan2019efficientnet},
8 input channels (window B then A; 4 bands per window), and 3 output
classes. We train with \texttt{logcoshdice} loss~\cite{milletari2016vnet,jadon2020losses},
class weights $[0.05,0.20,0.75]$ over the three classes, an ignore label
(index 3) applied to the pixels added when padding variable-size patches to
the $512\times512$ crop so they are excluded from the loss and metrics,
AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw}
at $10^{-3}$, 100 epochs, batch size 32 for B3 / 8 for B7, random
$512\!\times\!512$ crops, and bf16-mixed precision~\cite{micikevicius2018mixedprec}.
This gives a controlled S2-vs-Planet comparison before we add Planet-specific
regularization.

\subsection{Main result}

\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} isolates the dataset effect under the same
architecture and training recipe. With an EfficientNet-B3 backbone, replacing
Sentinel-2 with FTP improves PQ from 27.1 to 36.0, F1$_{[.5:.95]}$
from 21.0 to 29.1, and mean boundary error from 17.5m to 7.4m. FTP
B3 also outperforms the larger S2 EfficientNet-B7 baseline trained with the
same augmax recipe on PQ (36.0 vs 31.1), F1$_{[.5:.95]}$ (29.1 vs 24.7),
and boundary error (7.4m vs 15.2m); the S2 B7 model retains a slight edge
on polygon-count error ($|\Delta N|$ 54.9 vs 55.5). These comparisons use
object and polygon metrics because both sensors are scored after
vectorization from the same FTW polygon source. Our full-data
FTP-PRUE reaches $45.2$ object F1 on the 10-country dense held-out macro,
close to the best released S2 model's $47.0$ (FTW-PRUE (B7))~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}
despite a far smaller backbone. These two numbers are scored on different
evaluation sets --- our held-out macro vs.\ the released full-data test split ---
so we report the comparison for orientation only, not as a head-to-head
(full table in \Cref{tab:full_data}, appendix).

\subsection{Better augmentations}

\begin{figure}[t!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figs/aug_ablation_paper.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Cumulative augmentation lift on Obj F1} (FTP-PRUE B3, dense held-out, WS+TTA). Each step adds one augmentation group to the PRUE recipe. Horizontal lines mark the same-recipe FTW-PRUE \emph{augmax} baselines (solid: B3, architecture-matched; dashed: B7, strongest S2 model). The final FTP-PRUE model exceeds every S2 reference, so the dataset change drives the gain even at matched architecture.}
    \label{fig:aug_ablation}
\end{figure}

Data augmentations were the largest lever for PlanetScope performance
(\Cref{fig:aug_ablation}). We group the sweep into three stages so the
comparison is easy to audit: PRUE normalization/crop augmentations,
temporal/radiometric augmentations, and a final geometry/noise bundle.

\paragraph{PRUE normalization and crop augmentations.} The released PRUE configs enable
\texttt{preprocess\_aug} (random per-batch divisor in
$[5000, 15000]$ replacing the fixed $/10000$) and \texttt{resize\_aug}
(\texttt{RandomResizedCrop} scale $0.3\text{--}0.9$ to the training
crop). Adding them moves Obj F1 (WS+TTA) from $29.1 \rightarrow 31.9$.

\paragraph{Window swap and per-band gamma (+$1.0$ Obj
F1). \emph{augplus}.} Two cheap additions. \texttt{swap\_order}
randomly flips the A/B window ordering in the 8-channel stack per
sample; the FTW S2 datamodule already supported this but it was not enabled in our initial recipe.
Per-band random gamma ($\gamma \sim \mathcal{U}[0.8, 1.2]$, per channel
independently, $p\!=\!0.3$) models per-band atmospheric/radiometric
variation. We call this configuration \emph{augplus}.

\paragraph{Geometry/noise bundle (+$4.5$ Obj F1 over augplus). \emph{augmax}.}
Seven augmentations on top of \emph{augplus} form the final bundle. Per-band random affine
($y = ax + b$ per channel, $a \sim \mathcal{U}[0.9, 1.1]$,
$b \sim \mathcal{U}[-0.02, 0.02]$, $p\!=\!0.3$); Gaussian blur
($3\!\times\!3$ kernel, $\sigma \sim \mathcal{U}[0.5, 1.5]$,
$p\!=\!0.3$); Gaussian noise ($\sigma\!=\!0.015$, $p\!=\!0.3$);
single-window dropout ($p\!=\!0.15$ per batch to zero out either
window A or window B); small-angle rotation ($\pm 30^\circ$,
$p\!=\!0.5$, applied on top of the $90^\circ$-multiple rotation, H/V
flip, and random sharpness already present in the Kornia~\cite{riba2020kornia} base stack);
shear ($\pm 5^\circ$, $p\!=\!0.3$); and boundary-thickness jitter
(random binary dilation of the boundary class by $0\text{--}2$\,px,
$p\!=\!0.5$ per batch). The \emph{augmax} config additionally keeps
the two earlier baseline-fix augs enabled -- the PRUE
\texttt{preprocess\_aug} (random-divisor input normalization, modeling
cross-acquisition radiometric drift) and \texttt{RandomResizedCrop}
(scale $0.3\text{--}0.9$, ratio $0.75\text{--}1.33$, $p\!=\!0.5$).
Together on the 10-country dense held-out:
$32.9 \rightarrow 37.4$ Obj F1 (WS+TTA),
$56.4 \rightarrow 58.1$ pixel IoU.
The Obj F1 gain splits roughly evenly across precision and recall
($+3.4$\,pts each at the watershed F1). We call this \emph{augmax};
\emph{augplus} drops the seven bundle augmentations and keeps only
\texttt{swap\_order} and per-band gamma on top of the PRUE
normalization/crop stage.

\subsection{Sharper boundaries}

\paragraph{Watershed post-processing (+$0.4$--$0.8$\,pts Obj F1).}
Marker-controlled watershed with $h$-maxima seeds on a topographic
surface (the predicted SDF when an SDF head is present; otherwise the
Euclidean distance transform of the predicted boundary class) gives a
consistent gain across checkpoints.

\paragraph{D4 test-time augmentation (+$0.5$--$1.1$\,pts Obj F1 once
augmentations are on).} We use an 8-way flip+rotate ensemble~\cite{wang2019tta,moshkov2020tta}.
D4 TTA has little effect on the no-augmentation baseline but improves
models trained with \emph{augmax}.

\subsection{Stronger recipe}

\paragraph{Train on all 24 countries / 25 regions (+$7.8$\,pts Obj F1 over CC-BY).}
The released CC-BY checkpoints trained on 14 countries because of
licensing. We retrained on the full release; this lifts the B3 augmax model
from $37.4 \rightarrow 45.2$ Obj F1 (WS+TTA) on the 10-country dense
evaluation set. This is not a country-transfer claim; it measures the
full-data protocol used by the released full PRUE checkpoints.

\paragraph{Augmentations matter more than backbone scaling here.}
Scaling B3 to B7 gives a small lift on the CC-BY split
(\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics}) but hurts on the full-data split
($-1.6$ Obj F1; \Cref{tab:ablation_summary}). We therefore use B3 as
the main PlanetScope baseline.

\subsection{Metric choice}
\label{sec:polygon-first}

Field-boundary models are not consumed as rasters. Downstream
pipelines -- crop-yield estimation, irrigation accounting, subsidy
verification, parcel-level food-security monitoring -- ingest
\emph{vectorized parcels}. What these pipelines need is a polygon that
closes, separates from its neighbors, and lands in the right place
on the ground. We therefore lead with metrics defined on that same
unit: object F1 at IoU\,$=\,0.5$, panoptic
quality~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic} (PQ\,$=$\,SQ$\times$RQ, which
cleanly separates recognition from localization),
object F1 averaged over IoU thresholds $\{0.5,\ldots,0.95\}$~\cite{lin2014coco},
and a mean symmetric boundary chamfer in \emph{meters}.

Polygon-level metrics are less tied to raster-grid resolution. Sentinel-2
labels and PlanetScope labels are rasterized at different resolutions from
the same underlying polygon ground truth, so pixel IoU combines two
sources of variation -- bulk-area accuracy and a 1-pixel-wide boundary
class whose physical width differs by $3\times$ -- into a single
scalar. PQ, averaged F1, and meter-scale chamfer are defined on polygons and
are directly comparable: a $3$m boundary slip is $3$m whether
scored at $3$m or $10$m.

We retain pixel IoU as a secondary signal for continuity with the
PRUE protocol~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}. We do not use it as a headline
metric, and we do not interpret Kenya pixel IoU because the Kenya labels
are presence-only.

\paragraph{Pixel IoU is secondary.}
Pixel IoU remains useful for reproducing the PRUE protocol, but it is a
poor summary of field-boundary utility here. The metric is sensitive to
majority background, the physical width of the rasterized boundary class,
and country-specific annotation gaps. In Kenya, FTW labels are
presence-only, so treating unlabeled parcels as negatives makes pixel IoU
misleading. We therefore report pixel IoU only in the protocol-comparison
table and use polygon metrics for the main claims. A controlled
re-gridding check shows that re-projecting Planet predictions to the
$10$m S2 grid changes macro pixel IoU by $<0.1$
(\Cref{tab:gsd_controlled}, appendix).

\paragraph{Grid resolution vs.\ true resolution.}
A second, input-side control isolates how much of the Sentinel-2-to-PlanetScope
gap is genuine resolution rather than output grid. We retrain the Sentinel-2
model on imagery bilinearly upsampled to PlanetScope's $512\times512$ ($3$m)
grid --- same recipe, same field of view, no new image information --- and
score it through the full polygon pipeline (\Cref{tab:upsampled_s2_main}). The
finer grid recovers $\sim$45\% of the object-F1 / PQ gap to native PlanetScope
and lifts segmentation quality to near-parity ($77.5$ vs $77.9$): for the
parcels it matches, the overlap is as good as $3$m imagery. The boundary
chamfer, however, does \emph{not} improve --- it \emph{worsens} from $17.5$m to
$28.0$m, against $7.4$m for native PlanetScope --- even though the upsampled
predictions are scored on a finer grid that could in principle localize edges
more precisely. Output-grid resolution can inflate the object-detection scores,
but only genuine higher-resolution imagery delivers meter-scale boundary
precision. This is exactly why we report the boundary chamfer, in meters,
alongside the object metrics. Per-country numbers and the full experimental
setup are in \Cref{app:upsampled_s2}.

\paragraph{What the metrics are computed on.} All object metrics are
computed on the field-interior class after vectorization: predicted and
ground-truth field instances are matched, and PQ, the object F1 scores, and
the boundary chamfer are accumulated over those matches (definitions in
\Cref{sec:benchmark}). \Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} additionally reports the
polygon-count delta $|N_\text{pred} - N_\text{gt}|$, a simple over- vs
under-segmentation diagnostic, and \Cref{fig:metric_example} shows the
metrics on a worked example.

\paragraph{Per-region head-to-head.}
On the FTW \texttt{full\_data} protocol, macro PQ across the 23 regions
with complete polygon-metric outputs (all FTW regions except presence-only
Kenya and label-sparse India) is $38.3$ (Planet) vs $31.1$
(FTW-PRUE (B7), same recipe), a $+7.2$\,pp Planet win. Figure~\ref{fig:per_country_bars}
unpacks PQ into per-region deltas. Planet wins on $21/23$ regions and
loses two (two-sided sign test over the 23 decided regions:
$p \approx 6.6\times10^{-5}$).
The largest gains include Netherlands $+20.1$, Brazil $+15.5$,
Luxembourg $+15.3$, Lithuania $+13.7$, and Corsica $+10.6$.
The losses are Germany ($-7.0$\,pp) and Portugal ($-0.2$\,pp).
Germany is the clearest S2 win, and it is expected:
its fields are large and regular (median $3.5$\,ha, near the largest in the
release) and are already well resolved at $10$\,m, so PlanetScope's finer GSD
adds little detection signal. There, the Planet model's sharper, thinner
boundary predictions fragment the large parcels and lower its recognition and
segmentation quality (RQ $41$ vs $46$, SQ $80$ vs $86$) even though
its mean boundary error is actually \emph{lower} than S2's ($16.2$\,m vs
$19.0$\,m). Germany is precisely the regime --- large, sparse, geometrically
simple fields --- where higher resolution is least needed, which is the
mirror image of the smallholder regions where FTP gains most.

\paragraph{Does the 3m GSD advantage track field size?}
Across regions, $\Delta$\,PQ is only weakly correlated with per-region
median FTW field area (Pearson $r$ $=$ $+0.11$ on log-hectares, Spearman
$\rho$ $=$ $+0.15$, $n$ $=$ 22; scatter in \Cref{fig:smallholder_scatter},
appendix): the PQ gain is spread across regions and not tied to parcel size alone.
Small-field regions do anchor several top wins (Luxembourg $+15.3$\,pp at
$0.3$\,ha; Rwanda $+10.2$\,pp at $1.4$\,ha; Lithuania $+13.7$\,pp at
$1.4$\,ha), but so does large-field Netherlands
($+20.1$\,pp), whose PQ gain comes from sharper instance separation
rather than resolving sub-pixel parcels.
Germany is the one country that consistently favors S2 ($-7.0$\,pp): its
large, regular fields are already well resolved at $10$\,m, so resolution
adds little and the sharper Planet mask fragments large parcels
(detailed above). Cambodia is a modest Planet win on PQ ($+3.1$\,pp)
despite its monsoon-season imagery degradation
(\Cref{sec:limitations}).

\paragraph{Where higher resolution helps most.}
\Cref{fig:improvement} shows the held-out patches with the largest per-patch
object-F1 gain from Sentinel-2 to PlanetScope under the matched recipe. They
are uniformly dense smallholder scenes: the 10m model collapses adjacent
parcels into a few blobs or misses them entirely (per-patch object F1
$8$--$31$), while the 3m model recovers the parcel grid (object F1
$69$--$100$). These are the per-patch extremes, not the typical case: the
largest single-patch gains land on small, tightly-packed fields that are
sub-resolvable at 10m, even though aggregate per-region gain is only weakly
tied to field size (\Cref{fig:smallholder_scatter}).

\subsection{Ablation summary}

The full lever-by-lever recipe sweep --- including the rejected clDice,
SDF, frame-field, CutMix, and curriculum variants --- is consolidated in
\Cref{tab:ablation_summary} (appendix). The largest effects are the
augmentation bundle, full-data training ($+7.8$\,pp Obj F1), and, among the
rejected levers, the SDF auxiliary head ($-2.6$).

The main ablation result is that FTP needs stronger regularization than the S2 recipe: \textbf{augmax} adds +4.5 Obj F1 over \emph{augplus}, while CutMix, clDice, frame fields, and curriculum boundary dilation do not improve the final model. Full-data training adds +7.8 Obj F1 over the CC-BY split. The SDF auxiliary head helps without augmentations but hurts with \emph{augmax}, suggesting that the auxiliary task is redundant once the model is already regularized.

\section{Limitations and future work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Coverage.} 7{,}800 (5.5\%) of (patch, window) targets lack a PlanetScope companion in the released index, leaving a net yield of $94.5\%$ (133{,}168 of 140{,}968 targets; \Cref{tab:scope}). Because we release only patches with both seasonal windows, a patch missing either window is dropped in full. The dominant cause is activation failures that never produced a usable URL despite repeated reactivation; a smaller fraction extracted successfully but contained only low-quality imagery (heavy cloud, shadow, or unusable per UDM2) for which the resampling tool found no acceptable alternative scene within the season. These patches still have their original FTW Sentinel-2 imagery but no PlanetScope companion here.

\textbf{Selection bias of dropped patches.} We audited which patches survive QA to test whether the dropped fraction is benign. Per-patch retention (the fraction of a country's targets that survive both scene matching and QA, distinct from the usable-patch rate of \Cref{app:udm2_country}) is uneven across countries: Portugal (24\%, 66 patches), Finland (75\%), and the Netherlands (77\%) anchor the low end, while Croatia, Brazil, and Latvia exceed 96\%. Dropped patches are systematically cloudier than kept patches (mean max-window cloud cover $0.041$ vs. $0.024$; Mann-Whitney U two-sided $p < 10^{-300}$), with drop rate rising from $13\%$ in the cleanest cloud bin ($<1\%$) to $36\%$ in the cloudiest ($5\text{--}8\%$) within Planet's hard $10\%$ search filter. Conversely, dropped patches have \emph{lower} mean label density than kept patches ($0.276$ vs. $0.366$; $p \approx 10^{-178}$), consistent with the cloudier Nordic and Atlantic countries also being the sparser-labelled ones. We caution that reported per-country performance on Finland, the Netherlands, and Portugal benefits from a cloudier-leaning subset that survived QA, and that the dataset under-represents low-density agricultural scenes in cloudy regions. Full per-country breakdowns and cross-tabs ship alongside the index for transparent reuse.

\textbf{Temporal misalignment.} Our PlanetScope acquisition date is the closest cloud-free match to the FTW season midpoint, not to the exact Sentinel-2 acquisition date. The gap is usually under two weeks but can exceed a month in cloudy regions.

\textbf{Temporal generalization.} FTP ships exactly two seasonal windows per patch, drawn from the FTW v2 season definitions; we make no claim about multi-year or cross-year transfer. Models trained on this release should be re-evaluated before deployment on a different year's imagery.

\textbf{Spectral coverage.} The current release targets the 4-band Dove product (\texttt{ortho\_analytic\_4b\_sr}). 8-band SuperDove imagery would add red-edge and yellow bands that help vegetation discrimination, but FTW v2's labeled windows are historical and most predate SuperDove's full operational coverage, so the 4-band Dove archive is the only product with end-to-end coverage of the FTW season range. A SuperDove companion release is feasible only for the more recent country windows.

\textbf{Presence-only and hard transfer regions.} Kenya is presence-only in FTW: the tile annotates a subset of fields and leaves surrounding land unlabeled, so its background class is untrusted and supervised pixel/object metrics computed against it are not comparable across models. We therefore exclude Kenya from every supervised macro-average in this paper and report it only as a clearly marked presence-only row (\Cref{tab:heldout_pc}). Our evaluation tooling supports masking presence-only backgrounds to the ignore index at evaluation time, but the training labels still rasterize unlabeled Kenya land as background; a cleaner comparison should retrain with presence-only pixels masked out. Portugal remains a hard transfer case because its terraced micro-fields are near or below the PlanetScope GSD. These regions need either targeted data acquisition or a transfer-specific strategy.

\textbf{Forced monsoon acquisitions (Cambodia).} Cambodia is forced into monsoon-season PlanetScope acquisitions: the FTW v2 Cambodia window range overlaps only July--August 2021 PSScene coverage, with no dry-season fallback for the resampling tool to draw from. The result is the highest light-haze fraction of any region in the release ($3.7\times$ the global mean) and the lowest \texttt{clear} percentile. The model becomes high-precision / low-recall under that imagery (precision $47.5$, recall $29.4$), missing fields with diffuse boundaries. This is the primary driver of the Cambodia Obj F1 loss; on PQ and boundary chamfer Cambodia is still a Planet win (see \Cref{sec:polygon-first}).

\textbf{Societal impact.} Parcel-level field maps can be misused --- for example, by firms for farm-level surveillance or for insurance and credit profiling of individual growers, which would fall hardest on smallholders. We judge the benefits to global agricultural and food-security monitoring to outweigh these risks, and intend the labels and predictions for aggregate analysis rather than targeting individual farms.

\textbf{Future work.} Country-scale inference is the next practical test of FTP, especially in regions where small fields and dense parcel boundaries make 10m products brittle. We also plan to release trained PlanetScope checkpoints and investigate resolution-agnostic joint training that combines the Planet and Sentinel-2 versions of FTW in a single model. These directions are outside the scope of this paper.

\section{Conclusion}

Fields of the Planet (FTP) is a 3m PlanetScope companion to Fields of The World. It releases co-registered PlanetScope imagery, UDM2 quality masks, rasterized FTW labels, and the range-read pipeline used to build them. Under a matched PRUE recipe, FTP improves polygon-level field delineation over Sentinel-2 on PQ, F1$_{[.5:.95]}$, polygon-count error, and meter-scale boundary error. The result is a high-resolution FTW benchmark that keeps the original label protocol and splits while reducing the operational cost of working with PlanetScope imagery.

\section{Polygon-metric definitions}
\label{app:metrics}

All benchmark metrics are computed \emph{after vectorization}, on the
field-interior class only. This appendix gives the exact definitions
implemented in our evaluation code, so that every column of
\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} is reproducible from this text alone.

\paragraph{Vectorization.}
For a patch, let the predicted field-interior mask be the set of its
connected components, each traced to a simple polygon, giving a predicted
polygon set $\mathcal{P}=\{p_1,\ldots,p_M\}$; the ground-truth polygons form
$\mathcal{G}=\{g_1,\ldots,g_N\}$. All set operations below use polygon
\emph{areas} (via exact geometry), not pixel counts.

\paragraph{Polygon IoU and matching.}
For a ground-truth/prediction pair the intersection-over-union is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{IoU}(g,p)=\frac{\mathrm{area}(g\cap p)}{\mathrm{area}(g\cup p)}
=\frac{\mathrm{area}(g\cap p)}{\mathrm{area}(g)+\mathrm{area}(p)-\mathrm{area}(g\cap p)}.
\end{equation}
At an IoU threshold $\tau$, a prediction is a true positive iff it overlaps a
ground-truth polygon with $\mathrm{IoU}>\tau$. For $\tau\ge 0.5$ a polygon can
exceed $\tau$ with at most one polygon of the other set, so the matching
$\mathcal{M}_\tau\subseteq\mathcal{G}\times\mathcal{P}$ is a well-defined
partial bijection (greedy and optimal matching coincide). Writing
$\mathrm{TP}_\tau=|\mathcal{M}_\tau|$, $\mathrm{FP}_\tau=M-\mathrm{TP}_\tau$,
and $\mathrm{FN}_\tau=N-\mathrm{TP}_\tau$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Prec}_\tau=\frac{\mathrm{TP}_\tau}{\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\mathrm{FP}_\tau},\qquad
\mathrm{Rec}_\tau=\frac{\mathrm{TP}_\tau}{\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\mathrm{FN}_\tau},\qquad
\mathrm{F1}_\tau=\frac{2\,\mathrm{TP}_\tau}{2\,\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\mathrm{FP}_\tau+\mathrm{FN}_\tau}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Recognition, segmentation, and panoptic quality~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic}.}
With the match set fixed at $\tau=0.5$,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RQ}=\mathrm{F1}_{0.5},\qquad
\mathrm{SQ}=\frac{1}{\mathrm{TP}_{0.5}}\!\!\sum_{(g,p)\in\mathcal{M}_{0.5}}\!\!\mathrm{IoU}(g,p),\qquad
\mathrm{PQ}=\mathrm{SQ}\times\mathrm{RQ}.
\end{equation}
RQ is identically the object F1 at IoU\,$=0.5$ reported elsewhere in the
paper. Substituting shows this is the original panoptic quality,
$\mathrm{PQ}=\big(\sum_{(g,p)\in\mathcal{M}_{0.5}}\mathrm{IoU}(g,p)\big)\big/\big(\mathrm{TP}_{0.5}+\tfrac12\mathrm{FP}_{0.5}+\tfrac12\mathrm{FN}_{0.5}\big)$,
since $\mathrm{F1}_\tau=\mathrm{TP}_\tau/(\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\tfrac12\mathrm{FP}_\tau+\tfrac12\mathrm{FN}_\tau)$.

\paragraph{Threshold-averaged F1.}
With $\mathcal{T}=\{0.5,0.55,\ldots,0.95\}$ ($|\mathcal{T}|=10$)~\cite{lin2014coco},
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{F1}_{[.5:.95]}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}\sum_{\tau\in\mathcal{T}}\mathrm{F1}_\tau .
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Polygon-count error.}
Per patch we report $|\Delta N_\text{poly}|=|M-N|$, the absolute difference
between the number of predicted and ground-truth polygons, averaged over
patches. It is a coarse over/under-segmentation check independent of matching.

\paragraph{Symmetric boundary chamfer~\cite{cheng2021biou}.}
For each matched pair $(g,p)\in\mathcal{M}_{0.5}$, let $B_g$ and $B_p$ be the
one-pixel-thick boundary pixel sets of the two polygons. The symmetric
chamfer distance, in pixels, is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Ch}(g,p)=\frac{1}{2}\!\left(
\frac{1}{|B_g|}\sum_{x\in B_g}\min_{y\in B_p}\lVert x-y\rVert_2
\;+\;
\frac{1}{|B_p|}\sum_{y\in B_p}\min_{x\in B_g}\lVert y-x\rVert_2
\right),
\end{equation}
where each inner nearest-boundary distance is read from the Euclidean
distance transform of the opposing boundary. The boundary error in meters is
$\mathrm{Ch}(g,p)\cdot\text{GSD}$ (GSD\,$=3$\,m for PlanetScope). We report
its mean and 95th percentile over all matched pairs; pairs with an empty
boundary on either side are excluded. Only matched pairs contribute, so the
chamfer scores edge precision for the fields that were found (the same
scoping as SQ); missed and hallucinated fields are accounted for by RQ.

\paragraph{Pixel IoU (secondary).}
For continuity with the released PRUE protocol we also report the pixel-level
Jaccard index of the field-interior class, $\mathrm{IoU}_\text{px}=
|\hat{Y}\cap Y|/|\hat{Y}\cup Y|$ over all field-interior pixels, computed
before vectorization. Unlike the metrics above it has no notion of individual
parcels.

\paragraph{Aggregation.}
$\mathrm{TP}/\mathrm{FP}/\mathrm{FN}$ counts and matched IoUs are pooled
across all patches \emph{within} a country before forming that country's
RQ, SQ, PQ, and $\mathrm{F1}_{[.5:.95]}$; the chamfer is averaged over that
country's matched pairs. \Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} reports the macro-average
across the ten dense-label held-out countries (presence-only Kenya excluded;
see \Cref{sec:limitations}). Where a country has no predicted polygons under a
given configuration (Portugal under some CC-BY models), its boundary error is
undefined and that configuration's two boundary columns average the remaining
nine countries; every other column averages all ten.

\section{Access pipeline and operational lessons}
\label{app:pipeline}

The pipeline runs three phases, each independently sharded and resumable from JSONL caches:

\begin{enumerate}
\itemsep0em
    \item \textbf{Search.} Per (patch, window), call the Planet Data API with a date range, geometry filter, and cloud-cover ceiling. Output: best item\_id per patch.
    \item \textbf{Activate.} Deduplicate item\_ids globally; call \texttt{:activate} for SR and UDM2 in parallel. Output: signed Google Cloud Storage URLs.
    \item \textbf{Extract.} Group all (patch, window) by scene, open each COG once with \texttt{rasterio} via \texttt{/vsicurl/}, range-read every member's window, write a local GeoTIFF in PSScene's native UTM at 3m. No reprojection.
\end{enumerate}

All phases run on CPU nodes; no GPUs are used. We extract with windowed COG
range reads via \texttt{/vsicurl/}, which need only the Data API role and avoid
materializing full scenes through the Orders API.

\paragraph{Activation reliability.} Across 6{,}113 unique scenes, $\sim$24\% of
\texttt{:activate} calls returned URLs that failed with HTTP 400 on the first
range read. A second \texttt{:activate} call typically returned a working URL,
so the pipeline includes an automatic reactivation pass; this recovered 99\% of
the affected patches.

\section{Controlled GSD experiment}
\label{app:gsd_controlled}

\Cref{tab:gsd_controlled} reports the controlled re-gridding
experiment referenced in \Cref{sec:polygon-first}. For each model we
run inference on its native imagery, take the argmax, then
nearest-neighbour reproject the prediction onto the \emph{other}

\section{Upsampled-Sentinel-2 control}
\label{app:upsampled_s2}

The re-gridding check in \Cref{app:gsd_controlled} moves a fixed model's
predictions across grids; this is the complementary \emph{input-side}
control. We retrain the EfficientNet-B3 S2 model on Sentinel-2 imagery
bilinearly upsampled to PlanetScope's $512\times512$ ($3$m) grid --- identical
\emph{augmax} recipe, same field of view, no new image information --- and
score it through the full polygon pipeline (\Cref{tab:upsampled_s2}). Giving
the S2 model the finer output grid recovers roughly $45\%$ of its PQ and
object-F1 gap to native PlanetScope and brings segmentation quality to
near-parity ($77.5$ vs $77.9$): for the parcels it matches, the overlap is
as good as $3$m imagery.

Boundary precision tells the opposite story. The mean symmetric chamfer does
not improve --- it \emph{worsens} from $17.5$m to $28.0$m, against $7.4$m for
native PlanetScope --- even though the upsampled predictions are scored on a
finer $3$m grid that could in principle localize edges more precisely.
Upsampling lets the model detect and loosely overlap more small fields, but
their boundaries are interpolated from $10$m content and wobble; only genuine
$3$m imagery recovers meter-scale boundary accuracy. The absolute chamfer is
inflated by large-field countries (e.g.\ South Africa $44$m, Germany $39$m),
but the direction --- worse than native S2 despite a finer scoring grid --- is
unambiguous. This is direct evidence that object F1 / PQ are partly sensitive
to output-grid resolution while the boundary chamfer is not, and motivates
reporting both. (Training was stopped at $77/100$ epochs on a converged
val-loss plateau; we evaluate its best-val/loss checkpoint, matching the
protocol used for every other row.)
