\begin{abstract}
Fields of The World (FTW) makes global agricultural field delineation practical at scale, pairing field-boundary labels across 24 countries with public 10m Sentinel-2 imagery. But at 10m the smallest fields are often impossible to resolve as distinct parcels: rasterizing the same labels at 10m merges thousands of adjacent smallholder fields into single blobs, so a model cannot recover what the grid has already destroyed. We introduce \textbf{Fields of the Planet (FTP)}, a 3m PlanetScope companion to FTW built from the same field polygons, splits, and training recipe, with labels rasterized to each sensor's native grid. Because downstream use is parcel-level, the benchmark scores vectorized field polygons with panoptic quality, object F1, and meter-scale boundary error rather than pixel overlap. With task, geography, labels, architecture, and recipe all fixed, training on PlanetScope instead of Sentinel-2 lifts panoptic quality from 27.4 to 36.0 (0--100 scale) and more than halves mean boundary error (18.6m to 7.4m). Breaking results down by field size exposes the bottleneck: a $10$m grid can represent only $\sim$$8\%$ of sub-$0.5$\,ha fields as distinct polygons versus $\sim$$88\%$ at $3$m, and scored against the true polygons at each sensor's native resolution PlanetScope roughly doubles Sentinel-2's small-field polygon quality---a gap that upsampling Sentinel-2 to $3$m only partly closes, since interpolation cannot recover the boundaries that separate adjacent smallholder parcels. \footnote{Code, configs, result CSVs, and dataset-access instructions for review are available at \url{https://github.com/anon/anon} (anonymized); the full dataset is released on acceptance.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Field boundaries feed downstream pipelines for crop-yield estimation, irrigation accounting, subsidy verification, and global food-security monitoring~\cite{beckerreshef2020geoglam,defourny2019sen2agri}. They matter most where they are hardest to see: farms under 2\,ha account for roughly 84\% of the world's farms \emph{by count}~\cite{lowder2016farms,samberg2016smallholder}, and these smallholdings produce a disproportionate share of the food consumed in low- and middle-income regions. Their parcels are often only tens of meters across, so image resolution decides whether a boundary is visible at all. This tension between \emph{where boundaries matter most} and \emph{where they are hardest to resolve} motivates the two choices in this paper: higher-resolution imagery, and polygon-level metrics that test whether small fields are recovered as usable geometry.

A large public benchmark, \emph{Fields of The World} (FTW)~\cite{kerner2024ftw}, packages 1{,}627{,}378 parcel polygons across 24 countries and 25 labeled regions paired with 10m Sentinel-2 imagery~\cite{drusch2012sentinel2}. Ten meters approaches the resolution floor for smallholder delineation, and several authors~\cite{persello2019delineation,wang2022unlocking,garciapedrero2017boundary} have made this argument directly: fields on the order of tens of meters per side are difficult to delineate at S2 resolution. A sub-hectare parcel can span only a handful of 10m pixels, leaving its boundary indistinguishable from interior texture. The limit is baked into the labels: rasterizing FTW's own polygons onto the 10m grid already merges thousands of adjacent smallholder parcels into single connected blobs---only $\sim$$8\%$ of sub-$0.5$\,ha fields survive as distinct polygons at 10m versus $\sim$$88\%$ at 3m (\Cref{tab:representation_ceiling})---so a model trained on those 10m targets cannot recover them; the field has been merged before training begins. Existing high-resolution field-boundary datasets are either geographically narrower, use different label protocols, or are not aligned to FTW splits~\cite{wang2022unlocking,dandrimont2021parcel,estes2024africa,pastishd}.

PlanetScope is Planet Labs' constellation of $\sim$130 Dove CubeSats that images the entire land surface near-daily at $\sim$3m ground sample distance~\cite{planet2017}, roughly $3\times$ finer than Sentinel-2 and enough to place several pixels across the smallholder parcels that S2 cannot resolve. We pair PlanetScope's 3m surface-reflectance imagery with the FTW polygon ground truth, season windows, and tile-level splits, rasterizing the published FTW polygons onto each PlanetScope grid rather than collecting new labels. Each FTW patch has two seasonal windows, so the 70{,}484 FTW patches define 140{,}968 (patch, window) targets. FTP pairs 133{,}168 of them (94.5\%) with a cloud-screened PlanetScope acquisition, leaving 7{,}800 targets without a usable scene (\Cref{sec:limitations}).

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em
    \item A 3m PlanetScope companion to FTW across 24 countries / 25 regions: 66{,}584 patches, each pairing a planting- and harvest-season image (133{,}168 SR images) with a corresponding 3-class label mask rasterized from the FTW polygons.
    \item Per-patch UDM2 (Usable Data Mask 2) quality statistics and final QA decisions, so users can filter or audit imagery quality without re-reading the raw Planet assets.
    \item A reproducible, sharded access pipeline that assembles the release from 6{,}113 PlanetScope scenes.
    \item A benchmark built on the released FTW training recipe (PRUE~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}, extended to PRUE+) showing that, under matched architecture and recipe, FTP improves PQ, object F1, and meter-scale boundary error over Sentinel-2, with gains concentrated on the smallest (sub-$0.5$\,ha) fields.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Field-boundary benchmarks.}
AI4SmallFarms~\cite{persello2023ai4smallfarms} releases 50\,k smallholder parcels across South and Southeast Asia at 10m. PASTIS and PASTIS-HD provide panoptic agricultural parcel annotations for France with Sentinel-2 time series and aligned 1m SPOT 6/7 imagery~\cite{garnot2021pastis,pastishd}. Region- or country-scale high-resolution resources include PlanetScope labels for France and India~\cite{wang2022unlocking}, Africa-wide field-boundary labels digitized on NICFI Planet basemaps~\cite{estes2024africa}, and other national releases~\cite{persello2019delineation,dandrimont2021parcel}. FTW~\cite{kerner2024ftw} is broader geographically and already standardizes semantic, boundary, and instance masks across 24 countries / 25 regions. FTP keeps that label protocol and split structure while adding co-registered 3m imagery.

\paragraph{PlanetScope for field segmentation.}
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2022unlocking} use 3m PlanetScope for French and Indian parcels. Khallaghi et al.~\cite{khallaghi2025generalization} and Estes et al.~\cite{estes2024africa} use Planet basemap mosaics for smallholder cropland or field-boundary labeling; mosaics improve temporal coverage but can blur scene-level spatial detail. Waldner et al.~\cite{waldner2021decode} (DECODE) train field-boundary segmentation on PlanetScope over Australia, but their data and model weights are not released under a shared FTW-style protocol. \emph{Delineate Anything}~\cite{lavreniuk2025delineate} fine-tunes a YOLO11 instance segmenter~\cite{lavreniuk2025delineate} for field boundaries on a multi-sensor RGB mix spanning Sentinel-2 and higher-resolution (including PlanetScope) imagery; its zero-shot framing makes it useful as an off-the-shelf reference on FTW, not as a matched FTW-trained baseline. FTP keeps FTW's global labels, splits, and evaluation protocol while swapping only the imagery source.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

FTP reuses the existing FTW chips. For each chip and each of its two seasonal
Sentinel-2 windows, we query the Planet Data API for a single PlanetScope scene
(\texttt{ortho\_analytic\_4b\_sr}: 4-band surface reflectance, $\sim$3m,
uint16 Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFF) acquired inside the window and fully covering
the chip. We prefer the least-cloudy scene nearest the window midpoint; if none
covers the chip, that (patch, window) target is dropped.

Patches are written at native 3m on the PlanetScope grid, so their pixel
dimensions track the chip footprint (typically $\sim$510$\times$330\,px) rather
than the fixed 256$\times$256 S2 grid; we pad to fixed $512\times512$ crops for
training (\Cref{sec:benchmark}). We rank by scene-level cloud cover because the
UDM2 quality mask is only available after activation. After extraction, we
recompute UDM2 over each patch and resample cloudy patches from alternate
same-season scenes (\Cref{sec:quality}).

\textbf{Labels.}
\label{sec:labels}
We download the published FTW polygons (1{,}627{,}378 across all
regions)~\cite{ftw-source-coop} and rasterize them onto each PlanetScope patch
grid as FTW's 3-class mask: background, field interior, and a boundary class
buffered $3$m ($\approx$1 pixel per side) from each polygon's exterior ring,
which separates touching parcels that a binary mask would merge.

The grid resolution sets a hard ceiling on which fields can be represented as
distinct polygons at all, before any model is involved. Rasterizing the true FTW
polygons at each sensor's native resolution and vectorizing them back
(\Cref{tab:representation_ceiling}), a $10$m grid recovers only $7.5\%$ of
sub-$0.5$\,ha parcels as separable polygons versus $88.3\%$ at $3$m; medium and
large fields are largely unaffected. Because the parcels tile space (touching
neighbours), separating two adjacent small fields costs at least one boundary
pixel --- $10$m wide --- which erases them. This is an upper bound on \emph{any}
$10$m model and the core motivation for a $3$m companion to FTW.

\textbf{Scope and licenses.} FTP covers 66{,}584 of the 70{,}484 FTW patches
(we keep only patches with a PlanetScope match for both seasonal windows),
materializing 102\,GB from 6{,}113 scenes ($\sim$22 windows per scene);
\Cref{tab:scope} gives the full scope. The dataset and trained models are released on Huggingface under \textbf{CC-BY-NC 4.0}, inheriting Planet's non-commercial terms;
labels additionally carry their per-region FTW licenses. We release the training and evaluation code under a MIT license.

\section{Per-patch quality analysis}
\label{sec:quality}

We compute per-patch UDM2 statistics over the 129{,}490 released windows with non-null UDM2 statistics by reading the patch-area windows directly from each mask and counting positive pixels per band. \textbf{89.7\%} of those tiles are usable at a strict threshold (clear$\,\ge\,$95\%, unusable$\,\le\,$5\%); the per-class coverage breakdown is in \Cref{tab:udm2} (appendix). This is a per-image quality flag computed after pairing, a different denominator from the $94.5\%$ acquisition yield (fraction of targets with an extracted PlanetScope image, \Cref{sec:limitations}).

\paragraph{Patch-level resampling.}
The search filter rejects scenes whose \emph{scene-level} cloud cover exceeds
10\%, a coarse proxy because clouds can concentrate over a single patch even in
a low-cloud scene. The released index therefore uses a patch-level resampling
pass: for each patch failing any UDM2 threshold, we search alternative scenes
within the same season ($\pm$60\,d), probe UDM2 over the patch footprint, and
replace the SR + UDM2 pair when a candidate strictly improves quality. We ship
the tool and final QA table so users can reproduce the decisions or apply
stricter thresholds.

Per-country usable-patch rates vary widely (Portugal 48\%, Netherlands 79\%, vs Brazil/Croatia $>$99\%); full country breakdown in \Cref{app:udm2_country}.

\label{sec:benchmark}

We use PRUE~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue} as the benchmark recipe because it is the most directly comparable published FTW training setup. To isolate the imagery effect, we hold the architecture, loss, training schedule, and post-processing fixed across sensors, then compare Sentinel-2 and FTP with matched EfficientNet-B3 and EfficientNet-B7 U-Net baselines. \Cref{fig:qualitative} previews the final PlanetScope model against the S2 baseline.

\paragraph{How we measure (polygons first).} A pixel-overlap score can
look healthy while every small field in a patch is merged into its
neighbours, the failure that higher resolution is meant to fix. We therefore
score vectorized field interiors, asking whether a predicted field becomes a
usable parcel rather than a high-overlap raster blob.

\textbf{Object F1 at IoU\,$=0.5$} measures parcel detection: a predicted polygon
is a true positive when it overlaps a ground-truth polygon with IoU$>0.5$, and
F1 summarizes the resulting precision and recall. \textbf{Panoptic quality}
(PQ)~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic} multiplies this recognition quality by
segmentation quality, the mean IoU of matched parcels, so it separates finding a
field from fitting its shape.

\textbf{Object F1 averaged over IoU thresholds}
$\{0.5,0.55,\ldots,0.95\}$~\cite{lin2014coco} tightens the localization
criterion: loose matches count at low thresholds but disappear as the required
overlap approaches 0.95. \textbf{Mean symmetric boundary chamfer}~\cite{cheng2021biou}
reports the average predicted-to-ground-truth boundary distance in meters, so a
$3$m edge displacement is scored as $3$m regardless of pixel size. Formal
definitions are in \Cref{app:metrics}; \Cref{fig:metric_example} shows why
pixel IoU is kept only as a protocol-continuity metric.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.} We use the same per-region chip splits as the PRUE
paper~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}: we train and evaluate on the official Fields
of the World per-region train/validation/test chip splits. Within each region,
FTW partitions chips into disjoint train/validation/test sets; training sees
only \emph{train} chips and evaluation only the held-out \emph{test} chips, the
same protocol as PRUE. Our architecture-matched dataset comparison uses these
identical splits on both sensors. Kenya is the
\emph{only} presence-only region in FTW v2: it annotates a subset of fields
and leaves surrounding land unlabeled, so its background class is untrusted
and supervised pixel/object metrics computed against it are not comparable
across models. We therefore report all held-out macro-averages over the 10
dense-label countries, and Kenya appears only as a clearly marked
presence-only row (\Cref{tab:heldout_pc}, \Cref{sec:limitations}). Portugal
has trusted labels but remains a hard transfer case (terraced micro-fields
near the PlanetScope GSD); it stays in all metrics and is discussed in
\Cref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{PRUE-compatible recipe.} Unless otherwise stated, each model is
a U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} with an EfficientNet encoder~\cite{tan2019efficientnet},
8 input channels (window B then A; 4 bands per window), and 3 output
classes. We train with \texttt{logcoshdice} loss~\cite{milletari2016vnet,jadon2020losses},
class weights $[0.05,0.20,0.75]$ over the three classes, an ignore label
(index 3) applied to the pixels added when padding variable-size patches to
the $512\times512$ crop so they are excluded from the loss and metrics,
AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw}
at $10^{-3}$, 100 epochs, batch size 32 for B3 / 8 for B7, random
$512\!\times\!512$ crops, and bf16-mixed precision~\cite{micikevicius2018mixedprec}.

\paragraph{Model selection.} Per-model checkpoints are selected by minimum
\emph{validation} loss (FTW's validation chips; \texttt{ModelCheckpoint} on
\texttt{val/loss}), never the test chips. The PRUE+ augmentation recipe and
post-processing (watershed (WS), D4 test-time augmentation (TTA)) are fixed once
and applied identically to both sensors and all backbones. The sweeps in
\Cref{fig:aug_ablation,tab:heldout} characterize the recipe; the sensor
comparison uses the same recipe and selection rule on both sides.

\subsection{Main result}

\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} isolates the dataset effect under a fixed
architecture and recipe. At matched EfficientNet-B3, replacing Sentinel-2 with
FTP improves every polygon metric and more than halves the mean boundary error
(18.6m to 7.4m), lifting panoptic quality from 27.4 to 36.0. The PlanetScope B3
model also matches or beats the larger Sentinel-2 B7 baseline trained with the
same recipe on every polygon metric, so the gain is the imagery, not backbone
capacity. Both sensors are scored after vectorization from the same FTW polygon
source, so these are like-for-like object comparisons.

Our FTP-PRUE+ reaches 45.2 object F1 at IoU\,$=0.5$ --- identically the polygon
RQ column of \Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} --- close to the best released S2
model's 47.0 (FTW-PRUE B7~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}) despite a far smaller
backbone. The two use different evaluation sets and instance extractions
(watershed vs.\ connected components), so this is context rather than a
head-to-head comparison (full table in \Cref{tab:full_data}).

Augmentation was the largest PlanetScope recipe lever
(\Cref{fig:aug_ablation}). The sweep has three stages: the released PRUE
normalization/crop augmentations ($29.1 \rightarrow 31.9$ Obj F1), window swap
plus per-band gamma ($31.9 \rightarrow 32.9$), and a geometry/noise bundle that
defines \textbf{PRUE+} ($32.9 \rightarrow 37.4$). These four bars are trained on
the CC-BY countries and evaluated on held-out countries absent from training, so
the reported lift measures country transfer rather than within-country fit. The
final full-data FTP-PRUE+ model adds the remaining training countries on top of
the same recipe and reaches the headline 45.2 Obj F1. Exact augmentation ranges
appear with the ablation summary in the appendix.

\subsection{Sharper boundaries}

\paragraph{Watershed post-processing (+$0.4$--$0.8$\,pts Obj F1).}
Marker-controlled watershed with $h$-maxima seeds on a topographic
surface (the predicted SDF when an SDF head is present; otherwise the
Euclidean distance transform of the predicted boundary class) gives a
consistent gain across checkpoints.

\paragraph{D4 test-time augmentation (+$0.5$--$1.1$\,pts Obj F1 once
augmentations are on).} We use an 8-way flip+rotate ensemble~\cite{wang2019tta,moshkov2020tta}.
D4 TTA has little effect on the no-augmentation baseline but improves
models trained with PRUE+.

\subsection{Stronger recipe}

\paragraph{Augmentations matter more than backbone scaling here.}
Scaling B3 to B7 does not improve the dense held-out polygon metrics (PQ $36.0$
vs $36.0$, RQ $45.2$ vs $45.2$) and slightly worsens polygon-count error.
Across all $23$ regions, B7 is also marginally behind ($-0.2$ PQ). The largest
B3$\to$B7 drops are in dense-smallholder regions (Cambodia $-9.3$, Rwanda
$-6.5$, Vietnam $-5.1$\,points; distinct from the Planet$-$S2 deltas of
\Cref{sec:polygon-first}), so we use the smaller B3 as the main PlanetScope
baseline.

\subsection{Metric choice}
\label{sec:polygon-first}

Field-boundary outputs are consumed as vectorized parcels, not rasters. We
therefore score the fixed
\mbox{prediction\,$\rightarrow$\,post-processing\,$\rightarrow$\,vectorization}
pipeline that downstream users would run, applying the same watershed and D4
TTA to every model and sensor. Pixel IoU remains in the tables for continuity
with PRUE~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}, but it cannot distinguish the merge and
fragmentation failures shown in \Cref{fig:metric_example}.

\paragraph{Grid resolution vs.\ true resolution.}
An input-side control isolates how much of the Sentinel-2-to-PlanetScope
gap is genuine resolution rather than output grid. We give the Sentinel-2
model PlanetScope's $3$m grid with no new information --- first by upsampling
its input at evaluation ($\text{resize\_factor}{=}2$, our headline S2-B3 setup),
then by retraining on bilinearly upsampled imagery --- and score each through
the full polygon pipeline (\Cref{tab:upsampled_s2_main}). The object-level
scores climb steadily toward PlanetScope (PQ $23.8\!\to\!27.4\!\to\!31.5$ vs
$36.0$; SQ reaches near-parity, $77.5$ vs $77.9$): for the parcels it matches,
the overlap is as good as $3$m imagery. The boundary chamfer, however, never
tracks it: it stays $28$--$31$m against $7.4$m for native PlanetScope, even
though the upsampled predictions are scored on a finer grid that could in
principle localize edges more precisely. Output-grid resolution inflates the
object-detection scores, but only genuine higher-resolution imagery delivers
meter-scale boundary precision. We report boundary chamfer alongside the object
metrics; per-country numbers and the full setup are in \Cref{app:upsampled_s2}.

Breaking this by field size (\Cref{tab:resolution_ablation}, scored against true
polygons at each sensor's native ground resolution, macro-averaged over the dense
regions) sharpens the picture. Upsampling Sentinel-2 recovers part of the small-
and medium-field gap (PQ $7.5\!\to\!11.8$ and $28.4\!\to\!33.7$), but real $3$m
PlanetScope leads at every size. On large fields upsampling helps not at all
($36.1$ vs native $37.7$) --- they are already resolvable at $10$m --- so the
entire large-field gap to PlanetScope ($52.0$) is genuine resolution, not output
grid. The relative gap is largest on the smallest parcels ($2.1\times$ native
$10$m): you can interpolate a field's interior but not the sub-pixel boundaries
that separate adjacent smallholder parcels.

\paragraph{Per-region head-to-head.}
Across the 23 test regions with complete polygon-metric outputs (all FTW
regions except presence-only Kenya and label-sparse India), macro-average PQ is
$37.9$ for PlanetScope and $32.3$ for FTW-PRUE+ (B7) under the same recipe, a
$+5.6$\,points PlanetScope win. Figure~\ref{fig:per_country_bars} unpacks this into
per-region deltas and contrasts them with FTW's pixel-instance object F1.
PlanetScope wins on $18/23$ regions by polygon PQ but $11/22$ by
pixel-instance object F1, so the pixel-instance metric understates the
resolution benefit surfaced by polygon metrics. The PQ win count is a
directional consistency check across regions (two-sided sign test vs.\ a
$50\%$ win rate, $p \approx 1.1\times10^{-2}$), not an out-of-distribution
generalization test: under the PRUE protocol every region contributes both
train and test chips. All configurations use a single seed
(\texttt{seed\_everything}\,$=7$), so we report point estimates and do not
claim seed-level significance.

The largest gains are mid-size European regions. The five losses are Cambodia,
Germany, Portugal, Slovenia, and Vietnam, with the last three within $1.6$\,points
of a tie. Germany is the clearest Sentinel-2 win: at matched EfficientNet-B3 the
two sensors are within $\sim$2.9\,points (Planet $32.2$ vs S2 $35.1$), and most of
the larger headline gap comes from backbone capacity because B7 helps the S2
model on Germany but not Planet. The Planet model recognizes and localizes
Germany's large parcels less well than S2-B7 (RQ $40$ vs $46$, SQ $80$ vs
$86$) and tends to \emph{merge} adjacent parcels rather than fragment them. It
predicts about half as many polygons as there are parcels, while its field-area
accuracy (pixel IoU) is comparable; the boundaries there are often
low-contrast cadastral lines rather than physical features.

\paragraph{At the field level, the smallholder gain is real.}
Per-region averages are a confound: regions mix field sizes. Binning
\emph{individual} GT fields by area resolves it, but the binning must be
fair across sensors. We therefore score every model against the \emph{true}
FTW polygons (not the rasterized mask) at each sensor's \emph{native} ground
resolution---$10$m for Sentinel-2, $3$m for PlanetScope---so a $10$m model is
not credited for the sub-$10$m boundaries that an upsampled output grid lets it
paint but the sensor cannot resolve (\Cref{tab:resolution_ablation}). On this
honest footing PlanetScope leads at every size, and the relative gap is largest
on the smallest parcels: real $3$m roughly doubles Sentinel-2's small-field
polygon quality ($15.7$ vs $7.5$ PQ). Upsampling Sentinel-2 to $3$m recovers
part of the small/medium gap ($7.5\!\to\!11.8$) but none of the large-field gap
($36.1$ vs native $37.7$, against PlanetScope's $52.0$)---large fields are
already resolvable at $10$m, so that entire advantage is genuine resolution.
This is the deployed-pipeline quantity, and it tracks the model-free
representation limit (\Cref{tab:representation_ceiling}): you can interpolate a
field's interior but not the boundaries that separate adjacent smallholdings.

\subsection{Ablation summary}

The full lever-by-lever recipe sweep --- including the rejected clDice,
SDF, frame-field, CutMix, and curriculum variants --- is consolidated in
\Cref{tab:ablation_summary} (appendix). The largest effects are the
augmentation bundle and, among the rejected levers, the SDF auxiliary head
($-3.1$).

The main ablation result is that FTP needs stronger regularization than the S2
recipe: PRUE+ adds +4.5 Obj F1 over the lighter swap-order/gamma step, while
CutMix, clDice, frame fields, and curriculum boundary dilation do not improve
the final model. The SDF auxiliary head helps without augmentations but hurts
with PRUE+, suggesting that the auxiliary task is redundant once the model is
already regularized.

\section{Limitations and future work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Coverage and selection bias.} 7{,}800 (5.5\%) of (patch, window) targets
lack a PlanetScope companion (net yield $94.5\%$; \Cref{tab:scope}), mostly from
activation failures or patches with only low-quality imagery and no acceptable
same-season replacement; these keep their original FTW Sentinel-2 imagery. The
dropped fraction is not uniform: retention is lowest in cloudier countries
(Portugal $24\%$, Finland $75\%$, the Netherlands $77\%$), and dropped patches
are systematically cloudier and \emph{lower} label-density than kept ones.
Reported numbers for those regions therefore reflect a cloudier-leaning
surviving subset, and the release under-represents low-density scenes in cloudy
regions; full per-country cross-tabs ship with the index.

\textbf{Hard evaluation regions.} Kenya is the only presence-only FTW region, so
it is excluded from every supervised macro-average and reported only as a marked
row (\Cref{tab:heldout_pc}); a cleaner comparison would also mask its unlabeled
land during training. Portugal (terraced micro-fields near the PlanetScope GSD)
and Cambodia stay hard: Cambodia's FTW v2 windows overlap only monsoon-season
coverage, giving the release's highest haze fraction and a
high-precision/low-recall model that drives the Cambodia loss
(\Cref{sec:polygon-first}).

\textbf{Temporal and spectral scope.} The PlanetScope date is the closest
cloud-free match to the FTW season midpoint (usually within two weeks), and FTP
ships the two FTW v2 seasonal windows per patch, so we make no multi-year or
cross-year claim. The release targets the 4-band Dove product; 8-band SuperDove
would add red-edge/yellow bands but postdates most FTW v2 windows.

\textbf{Societal impact.} Parcel-level field maps can be misused, for example for
farm-level surveillance or credit profiling of individual growers, which would
fall hardest on smallholders. We intend the labels and predictions for aggregate
monitoring rather than targeting individual farms.

\textbf{Future work.} Country-scale inference is the next practical test of FTP,
and we plan to release trained PlanetScope checkpoints and explore
resolution-agnostic joint training over the Planet and Sentinel-2 versions of FTW.

\section{Conclusion}

Fields of the Planet (FTP) is a 3m PlanetScope companion to Fields of The
World. It releases co-registered PlanetScope imagery, per-patch UDM2 quality
statistics, rasterized FTW labels, and the range-read pipeline used to build
them. Under a matched PRUE recipe, FTP improves polygon-level field delineation
over Sentinel-2 on PQ, F1$_{[.5:.95]}$, and meter-scale boundary error while
keeping the original FTW label protocol and splits.

\section{Polygon-metric definitions}
\label{app:metrics}

All benchmark metrics are computed \emph{after vectorization}, on the
field-interior class only. This appendix gives the exact definitions
implemented in our evaluation code, so that every column of
\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} is reproducible from this text alone.

\paragraph{Vectorization.}
For a patch, let the predicted field-interior mask be the set of its
connected components, each traced to a simple polygon, giving a predicted
polygon set $\mathcal{P}=\{p_1,\ldots,p_M\}$; the ground-truth polygons form
$\mathcal{G}=\{g_1,\ldots,g_N\}$. All set operations below use polygon
\emph{areas} (via exact geometry), not pixel counts.

\paragraph{Polygon IoU and matching.}
For a ground-truth/prediction pair the intersection-over-union is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{IoU}(g,p)=\frac{\mathrm{area}(g\cap p)}{\mathrm{area}(g\cup p)}
=\frac{\mathrm{area}(g\cap p)}{\mathrm{area}(g)+\mathrm{area}(p)-\mathrm{area}(g\cap p)}.
\end{equation}
At an IoU threshold $\tau$, a prediction is a true positive iff it overlaps a
ground-truth polygon with $\mathrm{IoU}>\tau$. For $\tau\ge 0.5$ a polygon can
exceed $\tau$ with at most one polygon of the other set, so the matching
$\mathcal{M}_\tau\subseteq\mathcal{G}\times\mathcal{P}$ is a well-defined
partial bijection (greedy and optimal matching coincide). Writing
$\mathrm{TP}_\tau=|\mathcal{M}_\tau|$, $\mathrm{FP}_\tau=M-\mathrm{TP}_\tau$,
and $\mathrm{FN}_\tau=N-\mathrm{TP}_\tau$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Prec}_\tau=\frac{\mathrm{TP}_\tau}{\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\mathrm{FP}_\tau},\qquad
\mathrm{Rec}_\tau=\frac{\mathrm{TP}_\tau}{\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\mathrm{FN}_\tau},\qquad
\mathrm{F1}_\tau=\frac{2\,\mathrm{TP}_\tau}{2\,\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\mathrm{FP}_\tau+\mathrm{FN}_\tau}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Recognition, segmentation, and panoptic quality~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic}.}
With the match set fixed at $\tau=0.5$,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RQ}=\mathrm{F1}_{0.5},\qquad
\mathrm{SQ}=\frac{1}{\mathrm{TP}_{0.5}}\!\!\sum_{(g,p)\in\mathcal{M}_{0.5}}\!\!\mathrm{IoU}(g,p),\qquad
\mathrm{PQ}=\mathrm{SQ}\times\mathrm{RQ}.
\end{equation}
RQ is identically the object F1 at IoU\,$=0.5$ reported elsewhere in the
paper. Substituting shows this is the original panoptic quality,
$\mathrm{PQ}=\big(\sum_{(g,p)\in\mathcal{M}_{0.5}}\mathrm{IoU}(g,p)\big)\big/\big(\mathrm{TP}_{0.5}+\tfrac12\mathrm{FP}_{0.5}+\tfrac12\mathrm{FN}_{0.5}\big)$,
since $\mathrm{F1}_\tau=\mathrm{TP}_\tau/(\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\tfrac12\mathrm{FP}_\tau+\tfrac12\mathrm{FN}_\tau)$.

\paragraph{Threshold-averaged F1.}
With $\mathcal{T}=\{0.5,0.55,\ldots,0.95\}$ ($|\mathcal{T}|=10$)~\cite{lin2014coco},
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{F1}_{[.5:.95]}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}\sum_{\tau\in\mathcal{T}}\mathrm{F1}_\tau .
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Polygon-count error.}
Per patch the absolute count error is $|\Delta N_\text{poly}|=|M-N|$, the
difference between the number of predicted and ground-truth polygons. The raw
mean scales with parcel density --- a patch with hundreds of parcels dominates
the average --- so we report it normalized: for each country we divide the mean
per-patch $|\Delta N_\text{poly}|$ by the mean per-patch ground-truth count $N$,
then macro-average across countries ($|\Delta N|/N$). It is a coarse
over/under-segmentation check independent of matching.

\paragraph{Symmetric boundary chamfer~\cite{cheng2021biou}.}
For each matched pair $(g,p)\in\mathcal{M}_{0.5}$, let $B_g$ and $B_p$ be the
one-pixel-thick boundary pixel sets of the two polygons. The symmetric
chamfer distance, in pixels, is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Ch}(g,p)=\frac{1}{2}\!\left(
\frac{1}{|B_g|}\sum_{x\in B_g}\min_{y\in B_p}\lVert x-y\rVert_2
\;+\;
\frac{1}{|B_p|}\sum_{y\in B_p}\min_{x\in B_g}\lVert y-x\rVert_2
\right),
\end{equation}
where each inner nearest-boundary distance is read from the Euclidean
distance transform of the opposing boundary. The boundary error in meters is
$\mathrm{Ch}(g,p)\cdot\text{GSD}$ (GSD\,$=3$\,m for PlanetScope). We report
its mean and 95th percentile over all matched pairs; pairs with an empty
boundary on either side are excluded. Only matched pairs contribute, so the
chamfer scores edge precision for the fields that were found (the same
scoping as SQ); missed and hallucinated fields are accounted for by RQ.

\paragraph{Pixel IoU (secondary).}
For continuity with the released PRUE protocol we also report the pixel-level
Jaccard index of the field-interior class, $\mathrm{IoU}_\text{px}=
|\hat{Y}\cap Y|/|\hat{Y}\cup Y|$ over all field-interior pixels, computed
before vectorization. Unlike the metrics above it has no notion of individual
parcels.

\paragraph{Aggregation.}
$\mathrm{TP}/\mathrm{FP}/\mathrm{FN}$ counts and matched IoUs are pooled
across all patches \emph{within} a country before forming that country's
RQ, SQ, PQ, and $\mathrm{F1}_{[.5:.95]}$; the chamfer is averaged over that
country's matched pairs. \Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} reports the macro-average
across the ten dense-label held-out countries (presence-only Kenya excluded;
see \Cref{sec:limitations}). Where a country has no predicted polygons under a
given configuration (e.g.\ Portugal under the weaker S2 models), its boundary error is
undefined and that configuration's two boundary columns average the remaining
nine countries; every other column averages all ten.

\paragraph{Field-area breakdown (\Cref{tab:resolution_ablation}).}
To localize \emph{where} resolution helps, we also report the polygon metrics
per field-size bin. Each ground-truth polygon is assigned by its area to
\emph{small} ($<0.5$\,ha), \emph{medium} ($0.5$--$2$\,ha), or \emph{large}
($>2$\,ha); the $2$\,ha cut is the standard smallholder ceiling~\cite{lowder2016farms}
and $0.5$\,ha splits the dominant sub-hectare mass (median field $=0.41$\,ha).
A matched / missed ground-truth polygon is
credited to the bin of \emph{its} area; a false positive to the bin of the
predicted polygon's area. The per-bin columns are the same quantities defined
above evaluated within a bin: $\mathrm{RQ}_{.5}=\mathrm{F1}_{0.5}$ (detection),
$\mathrm{F1}_{0.75}$, $\mathrm{AP}=\mathrm{F1}_{[.5:.95]}$ (localization), and
$\mathrm{PQ}=\mathrm{SQ}\times\mathrm{RQ}_{.5}$. Unlike the per-country
aggregation, per-bin $\mathrm{TP}/\mathrm{FP}/\mathrm{FN}$ and matched IoUs are
pooled (micro) across \emph{all} dense-label patches before forming each bin's
metrics, so sparsely-populated bins stay stable; the ``All'' row pools every
field. Because area in hectares is grid-invariant, the bins are comparable
across sensors even though the $10$m and $3$m label rasterizations resolve
different numbers of small fields.

\section{Upsampled-Sentinel-2 control}
\label{app:upsampled_s2}

The upsampled-Sentinel-2 control isolates genuine resolution from the evaluation
grid. We retrain the EfficientNet-B3 S2 model on Sentinel-2 imagery bilinearly
upsampled to PlanetScope's $512\times512$ ($3$m) grid with the identical PRUE+
recipe, same field of view, and no new image information, then score it through
the full polygon pipeline (\Cref{tab:upsampled_s2}). The finer output grid
recovers roughly $45\%$ of the PQ and object-F1 gap to native PlanetScope and
brings segmentation quality to near-parity ($77.5$ vs $77.9$): for the parcels
it matches, the overlap is as good as $3$m imagery.

Boundary precision tells the opposite story. The mean symmetric chamfer does
not improve --- it \emph{worsens} from $18.6$m to $28.0$m, against $7.4$m for
native PlanetScope --- even though the upsampled predictions are scored on a
finer $3$m grid that could in principle localize edges more precisely.
Upsampling lets the model detect and loosely overlap more small fields, but
their boundaries are interpolated from $10$m content and wobble; only genuine
$3$m imagery recovers meter-scale boundary accuracy. The absolute chamfer is
inflated by large-field countries (e.g.\ South Africa $44$m, Germany $39$m),
but the direction --- worse than native S2 despite a finer scoring grid --- is
consistent across configurations. Object F1 and PQ are therefore partly
sensitive to output-grid resolution, while boundary chamfer is not; we report
both. Training stopped at $77/100$ epochs on a converged validation-loss
plateau, and we evaluate its best-val/loss checkpoint as in the other rows.
