\begin{abstract}
Fields of The World (FTW) provides field-boundary labels across 24 countries with public 10m Sentinel-2 imagery. However, 10m-per-pixel imagery is too coarse to separate many of the smallest fields, especially smallholder parcels. We introduce \textbf{Fields of the Planet (FTP)}, a 3m PlanetScope companion to FTW built from the same field polygons, splits, and training recipe, where the vector field labels are rasterized to the sensor's native grid. Because downstream use of field boundaries is parcel-level, the benchmark scores vectorized field polygon predictions with panoptic quality, object F1, and meter-scale boundary error rather than pixel IoU. With task, geography, labels, architecture, and recipe all fixed, training on PlanetScope instead of Sentinel-2 increases panoptic quality from 21.0 to 35.5 (0--100 scale) and more than halves matched-polygon mean boundary error (18.6m to 7.4m). The field-size analysis shows where the resolution limit appears: a $10$m grid can represent only $\sim$$7.5\%$ of sub-$0.5$\,ha fields as distinct polygons versus $\sim$$88\%$ at $3$m. When both sensors are scored against the true polygons at their native resolutions, PlanetScope roughly doubles Sentinel-2's small-field polygon quality. Upsampling Sentinel-2 to $3$m closes only part of the gap. \footnote{Code, configs, result CSVs, and dataset-access instructions for review are available at \url{https://github.com/anon/anon} (anonymized); the full dataset is released on acceptance.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Field boundaries feed downstream pipelines for crop-yield estimation, irrigation accounting, subsidy verification, and global food-security monitoring~\cite{beckerreshef2020geoglam,defourny2019sen2agri}. They matter most where they are hardest to see: farms under 2\,ha account for roughly 84\% of the world's farms \emph{by count}~\cite{lowder2016farms,samberg2016smallholder}, and these smallholdings produce a large share of the food consumed in low- and middle-income regions. Their parcels are often only tens of meters across, so image resolution decides whether a boundary is visible at all. This tension between \emph{where boundaries matter most} and \emph{where they are hardest to resolve} motivates the two choices in this paper: higher-resolution imagery, and polygon-level metrics that test whether small fields are recovered as usable geometry.

A large public benchmark, \emph{Fields of The World} (FTW)~\cite{kerner2024ftw}, packages 1{,}627{,}378 parcel polygons across 24 countries and 25 labeled regions paired with 10m Sentinel-2 imagery~\cite{drusch2012sentinel2}. A $10$m-per-pixel image is often too coarse for accurate smallholder field delineation, and prior work makes the same point directly: fields on the order of tens of meters per side are difficult to delineate at S2 resolution~\cite{persello2019delineation,wang2022unlocking,garciapedrero2017boundary}. A sub-hectare parcel can span only a handful of 10m pixels, leaving its boundary indistinguishable from interior texture. The limitation appears before model training: rasterizing FTW's own polygons onto the 10m grid already merges thousands of adjacent smallholder parcels into single connected blobs. Only $\sim$$7.5\%$ of sub-$0.5$\,ha fields remain separated as distinct polygons at 10m versus $\sim$$88\%$ at 3m (\Cref{tab:representation_ceiling}), so a model trained on those 10m targets cannot recover them. The parcels are already merged in the labels before training begins. Existing high-resolution field-boundary datasets are either geographically narrower, use different label protocols, or are not aligned to FTW splits~\cite{wang2022unlocking,dandrimont2021parcel,estes2024africa,pastishd}.

PlanetScope is Planet Labs' constellation of $\sim$130 Dove CubeSats that images the entire land surface near-daily at $\sim$3m ground sample distance~\cite{planet2017}, roughly $3\times$ finer than Sentinel-2 and enough to place several pixels across the smallholder parcels that S2 cannot resolve. We pair PlanetScope's 3m surface-reflectance imagery with the FTW polygon ground truth, season windows, and tile-level splits, rasterizing the published FTW polygons onto each PlanetScope grid rather than collecting new labels. Each FTW patch has two seasonal windows, so the 70{,}484 FTW patches define 140{,}968 (patch, window) targets. FTP pairs 133{,}168 of them (94.5\%) with a cloud-screened PlanetScope acquisition, leaving 7{,}800 targets without a usable scene (\Cref{sec:limitations}).

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em
    \item A 3m PlanetScope companion to FTW across 24 countries / 25 regions: 66{,}584 patches, each pairing a planting- and harvest-season image (133{,}168 SR images) with a corresponding 3-class label mask rasterized from the FTW polygons. The dataset and trained models are released under CC-BY-NC 4.0, with labels retaining their per-region FTW licenses.
    \item Per-patch UDM2 (Usable Data Mask 2) quality statistics and final QA decisions, so users can filter or audit imagery quality.
    \item A reproducible pipeline that assembles the release from 6{,}113 raw PlanetScope scenes.
    \item A training recipe, PRUE+, that extends the PRUE training recipe~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue} with results showing that, under matched architecture and recipe, FTP improves PQ, object F1, and matched-polygon meter-scale boundary error over Sentinel-2, with gains concentrated on the smallest (sub-$0.5$\,ha) fields.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Field-boundary benchmarks.}
AI4SmallFarms~\cite{persello2023ai4smallfarms} releases 50\,k smallholder parcels across South and Southeast Asia at 10m. PASTIS and PASTIS-HD provide panoptic agricultural parcel annotations for France with Sentinel-2 time series and aligned 1m SPOT 6/7 imagery~\cite{garnot2021pastis,pastishd}. Region- or country-scale high-resolution resources include PlanetScope labels for France and India~\cite{wang2022unlocking}, Africa-wide field-boundary labels digitized on NICFI Planet basemaps~\cite{estes2024africa}, and other national releases~\cite{persello2019delineation,dandrimont2021parcel}. FTW~\cite{kerner2024ftw} is broader geographically and already standardizes semantic, boundary, and instance masks across 24 countries / 25 regions. FTP keeps that label protocol and split structure while adding co-registered 3m imagery.

\paragraph{PlanetScope for field segmentation.}
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2022unlocking} use 3m PlanetScope for French and Indian parcels. Khallaghi et al.~\cite{khallaghi2025generalization} and Estes et al.~\cite{estes2024africa} use Planet basemap mosaics for smallholder cropland or field-boundary labeling; mosaics improve temporal coverage but can blur scene-level spatial detail. Waldner et al.~\cite{waldner2021decode} (DECODE) train field-boundary segmentation on PlanetScope over Australia, but their data and model weights are not released under a shared FTW-style protocol. \emph{Delineate Anything}~\cite{lavreniuk2025delineate} fine-tunes a YOLO11 instance segmenter for field boundaries on a multi-sensor RGB mix spanning Sentinel-2 and higher-resolution (including PlanetScope) imagery; its zero-shot framing makes it useful as an off-the-shelf reference on FTW, not as a matched FTW-trained baseline. FTP keeps FTW's global labels, splits, and evaluation protocol while swapping only the imagery source.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

FTP reuses the existing FTW chips. For each chip and each of its two seasonal
Sentinel-2 windows, we query the Planet Data API for a single PlanetScope scene
(\texttt{ortho\_analytic\_4b\_sr}: 4-band surface reflectance, $\sim$3m,
uint16 Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFF) acquired inside the window and fully covering
the chip. We prefer the least-cloudy scene nearest the window midpoint; if none
covers the chip, that (patch, window) target is dropped.

Patches are written at native 3m on the PlanetScope grid, so their pixel
dimensions track the chip footprint (typically $\sim$510$\times$330\,px) rather
than the fixed 256$\times$256 S2 grid. During training, we sample
$512\times512$ windows and add ignore-labeled padding only when a native patch
is smaller than the training window (\Cref{sec:benchmark}). We rank by scene-level cloud cover because the
UDM2 quality mask is only available after activation. After extraction, we
recompute UDM2 over each patch and resample cloudy patches from alternate
same-season scenes (\Cref{sec:quality}).

\textbf{Labels.}
\label{sec:labels}
We download the published FTW polygons (1{,}627{,}378 across all
regions)~\cite{ftw-source-coop} and rasterize them onto each PlanetScope patch
grid as FTW's 3-class mask: background, field interior, and a boundary class
buffered $3$m ($\approx$1 pixel per side) from each polygon's exterior ring,
which separates touching parcels that a binary mask would merge.

The grid resolution determines which fields can be represented as distinct
polygons before any model is involved. Rasterizing the true FTW
polygons at each sensor's native resolution and vectorizing them back
(\Cref{tab:representation_ceiling}), a $10$m grid recovers only $7.5\%$ of
sub-$0.5$\,ha parcels as separable polygons versus $88.3\%$ at $3$m; medium and
large fields are largely unaffected. Adjacent parcels can remain separate in a
raster label only if at least one boundary pixel fits between them. At $10$m,
that boundary pixel can be wider than the gap between small parcels, so
neighboring fields merge. This is an upper bound for models trained and
evaluated through this raster-label pipeline, and the core motivation for a
$3$m companion to FTW.

\textbf{Scope and licenses.} FTP covers 66{,}584 of the 70{,}484 FTW patches
(we keep only patches with a PlanetScope match for both seasonal windows),
materializing 102\,GB from 6{,}113 scenes ($\sim$22 windows per scene);
\Cref{tab:scope} gives the full scope. The dataset and trained models are released on Hugging Face under \textbf{CC-BY-NC 4.0}, inheriting Planet's non-commercial terms;
labels additionally carry their per-region FTW licenses. We release the training and evaluation code under an MIT license.

\section{Per-patch quality analysis}
\label{sec:quality}

For the 129{,}490 released windows with non-null UDM2 statistics, we compute per-patch coverage by reading the patch-area window from each mask and counting positive pixels per band. \textbf{89.7\%} of those tiles are usable at a strict threshold (clear$\,\ge\,$95\%, unusable$\,\le\,$5\%); the per-class coverage breakdown is in \Cref{tab:udm2} (appendix). This per-image quality flag is computed after pairing, so its denominator differs from the $94.5\%$ acquisition yield (fraction of targets with an extracted PlanetScope image, \Cref{sec:limitations}).

\paragraph{Patch-level resampling.}
The search filter rejects scenes whose \emph{scene-level} cloud cover exceeds
10\%, a coarse proxy because clouds can concentrate over a single patch even in
a low-cloud scene. The released index therefore uses a patch-level resampling
pass: for each patch failing any UDM2 threshold, we search alternative scenes
within the same season ($\pm$60\,d), probe UDM2 over the patch footprint, and
replace the SR + UDM2 pair when a candidate strictly improves quality. We ship
the tool and final QA table so users can reproduce the decisions or apply
stricter thresholds.

Per-country usable-patch rates vary widely (Portugal 48\%, Netherlands 79\%, Brazil/Croatia $>$99\%); the full country breakdown is in \Cref{app:udm2_country}.

\section{Building a PlanetScope baseline}
\label{sec:benchmark}

We use PRUE~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue} as the benchmark recipe because it is the most directly comparable published FTW training setup. To isolate the imagery effect, we hold the architecture, loss, training schedule, and post-processing fixed across sensors, then compare Sentinel-2 and FTP with matched EfficientNet-B3 and EfficientNet-B7 U-Net baselines. \Cref{fig:qualitative} previews the final PlanetScope model against the S2 baseline.

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics.} A pixel-overlap score can
remain high even when every small field in a patch is merged into its
neighbours. We therefore
score vectorized field interiors, asking whether a predicted field becomes a
usable parcel rather than a high-overlap raster blob.

\textbf{Object F1 at IoU\,$=0.5$} measures parcel detection: a predicted polygon
is a true positive when it overlaps a ground-truth polygon with IoU$>0.5$, and
F1 summarizes the resulting precision and recall. \textbf{Panoptic quality}
(PQ)~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic} multiplies this recognition quality by
segmentation quality, the mean IoU of matched parcels, so it separates finding a
field from fitting its shape.

\textbf{Object F1 averaged over IoU thresholds}
$\{0.5,0.55,\ldots,0.95\}$~\cite{lin2014coco} tightens the localization
criterion: loose matches count at low thresholds but disappear as the required
overlap approaches 0.95. \textbf{Mean symmetric boundary chamfer}~\cite{cheng2021biou}
reports the average predicted-to-ground-truth boundary distance in meters for
matched polygons, so a $3$m edge displacement is scored as $3$m regardless of
pixel size. Missed and hallucinated fields are counted by RQ/F1 rather than
chamfer. Formal definitions are in \Cref{app:metrics}; \Cref{fig:metric_example}
shows why pixel IoU is kept only as a protocol-continuity metric.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.} We use the same per-region chip splits as the PRUE
paper~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}: we train and evaluate on the official Fields
of the World per-region train/validation/test chip splits. Within each region,
FTW partitions chips into disjoint train/validation/test sets; training sees
only \emph{train} chips and evaluation only the held-out \emph{test} chips, the
same protocol as PRUE. Our architecture-matched dataset comparison uses these
identical splits on both sensors. Kenya is the
\emph{only} presence-only region in FTW v2: it annotates a subset of fields
and leaves surrounding land unlabeled, so its background class is untrusted
and supervised pixel/object metrics computed against it are not comparable
across models. We therefore report all held-out macro-averages over the 10
dense-label countries and exclude Kenya, which is discussed separately
(\Cref{sec:limitations}). Portugal
has trusted labels, but transfer remains difficult there because many terraced
micro-fields are close to PlanetScope's ground sample distance; it stays in all
metrics and is discussed in
\Cref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{PRUE-compatible recipe.} Unless otherwise stated, each model is
a U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} with an EfficientNet encoder~\cite{tan2019efficientnet},
8 input channels (window B then A; 4 bands per window), and 3 output
classes. We train with \texttt{logcoshdice} loss~\cite{milletari2016vnet,jadon2020losses},
class weights $[0.05,0.20,0.75]$ over the three classes, an ignore label
(index 3) applied only to padded pixels in patches smaller than the
$512\times512$ training window so they are excluded from the loss and metrics,
AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw}
at $10^{-3}$, 100 epochs, batch size 32 for B3 / 8 for B7, random
$512\!\times\!512$ crops, and bf16-mixed precision~\cite{micikevicius2018mixedprec}.

\paragraph{Model selection.} Per-model checkpoints are selected by minimum
\emph{validation} loss (FTW's validation chips; \texttt{ModelCheckpoint} on
\texttt{val/loss}), never the test chips. The PRUE+ augmentation recipe and
post-processing (watershed (WS), D4 test-time augmentation (TTA)) are fixed once
and applied identically to both sensors and all backbones. The sweeps in
\Cref{fig:aug_ablation,tab:heldout} characterize the recipe; the sensor
comparison uses the same recipe and selection rule on both sides.

\subsection{Main result}

\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} isolates the imagery effect under a fixed
architecture and recipe. With matched EfficientNet-B3 backbones, replacing Sentinel-2 with
FTP improves the main polygon metrics and more than halves matched-polygon mean boundary error
(18.6m to 7.4m), raising panoptic quality from 21.0 to 35.5. The PlanetScope B3
model also matches or beats the larger Sentinel-2 B7 baseline trained with the
same recipe on the main polygon metrics, so the gain is the imagery, not backbone
capacity. Both sensors are scored after vectorization from the same FTW polygon
source, so these are like-for-like object comparisons.

Our FTP-PRUE+ reaches 46.2 object F1 at IoU\,$=0.5$ --- the same quantity as the polygon
RQ column of \Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} --- close to the best released S2
model's 47.0 (FTW-PRUE B7~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}) despite a far smaller
backbone. The two use different evaluation sets and instance extractions
(watershed vs.\ connected components), so this is context rather than a
head-to-head comparison (full table in \Cref{tab:heldout}).

Augmentation had the largest effect in the PlanetScope recipe
(\Cref{fig:aug_ablation}). The sweep has three stages: the released PRUE
normalization/crop augmentations ($29.1 \rightarrow 31.9$ Obj F1), window swap
plus per-band gamma ($31.9 \rightarrow 32.9$), and a geometry/noise bundle that
defines \textbf{PRUE+} ($32.9 \rightarrow 37.4$). These four bars are trained on
the CC-BY countries and evaluated on held-out countries absent from training, so
the reported improvement measures country transfer rather than within-country fit. The
final full-data FTP-PRUE+ model adds the remaining training countries on top of
the same recipe, reaching 46.2 polygon RQ
(\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics}); the matching watershed-instance Obj F1 plotted
here is 45.2. Exact augmentation ranges are listed with the ablation summary in the
appendix.

\subsection{Boundary post-processing}

\paragraph{Watershed post-processing.}
Marker-controlled watershed with $h$-maxima seeds on a topographic
surface (the predicted SDF when an SDF head is present; otherwise the
Euclidean distance transform of the predicted boundary class) gives a
consistent $+0.4$--$0.8$\,pt Obj F1 gain across checkpoints.

\paragraph{D4 test-time augmentation.} We use an 8-way flip+rotate
ensemble~\cite{wang2019tta,moshkov2020tta}. D4 TTA has little effect on the
no-augmentation baseline but improves models trained with PRUE+ by
$+0.5$--$1.1$\,pts Obj F1.

\subsection{Backbone scaling}

Scaling B3 to B7 does not improve the dense held-out polygon metrics (PQ $35.5$
vs $35.4$, RQ $46.2$ vs $46.1$). Across all $23$ regions, B7 is also marginally
behind ($-0.2$ PQ). The largest
B3$\to$B7 drops are in dense-smallholder regions (Cambodia $-9.3$, Rwanda
$-6.5$, Vietnam $-5.1$\,points; distinct from the Planet$-$S2 deltas of
\Cref{sec:polygon-first}), so we use the smaller B3 as the main PlanetScope
baseline.

\subsection{Resolution and field-size analysis}
\label{sec:polygon-first}

Field-boundary outputs are consumed as vectorized parcels, not rasters. We
therefore score the fixed
\mbox{prediction\,$\rightarrow$\,post-processing\,$\rightarrow$\,vectorization}
pipeline that downstream users would run, applying the same watershed and D4
TTA to every model and sensor. Pixel IoU remains in the tables for continuity
with PRUE~\cite{muhawenayo2026prue}, but it cannot distinguish the merge and
fragmentation failures shown in \Cref{fig:metric_example}.

The main native-resolution comparisons (\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} and the
per-region figures) score against the \emph{true} FTW polygons with Sentinel-2
capped to 10m and PlanetScope at 3m. The upsampled-grid controls in
\Cref{tab:resolution_ablation,tab:upsampled_s2_main} are separate output-grid
checks, so the same S2-B3 model has different absolute PQ values across protocols
($21.0$ vs $23.8$); we never mix protocols within a single comparison.

\paragraph{Grid resolution vs.\ image resolution.}
An input-side control tests how much of the Sentinel-2-to-PlanetScope gap comes
from output grid rather than image content. We give the Sentinel-2 model
a finer 512-pixel output grid with no new information --- first by upsampling its
input at evaluation ($\text{resize\_factor}{=}2$, our headline S2-B3 setup),
then by retraining on bilinearly upsampled imagery --- and score each through
the full polygon pipeline (\Cref{tab:upsampled_s2_main}). The object-level
scores move steadily toward PlanetScope (PQ $23.8\!\to\!27.4\!\to\!31.5$ vs
$36.0$; SQ nearly matches, $77.5$ vs $77.9$): for the parcels it matches,
the overlap approaches the $3$m imagery result. Boundary chamfer, however, is
reported with the S2-native 10m GSD for S2 rows and stays $28$--$31$m against
$7.4$m for native PlanetScope. Output-grid upsampling does not recover the
boundary precision obtained with native $3$m imagery, so we report boundary
chamfer alongside the object metrics.

The field-size breakdown (\Cref{tab:resolution_ablation}) shows where the
resolution difference matters most: real $3$m PlanetScope leads at every size,
while upsampling Sentinel-2 recovers only the small and medium fields and does
not improve performance on large fields (they are already resolvable at $10$m). The relative gap is
largest on the smallest parcels --- a model can interpolate a field's interior but
not the sub-pixel boundaries that separate adjacent smallholder parcels. The
per-bin numbers and binning protocol are in the field-size breakdown below.

\paragraph{Per-region comparison.}
Across the 23 test regions with complete polygon-metric outputs (all FTW
regions except presence-only Kenya and label-sparse India), and scoring both
sensors against the true polygons at their native resolution (Planet $3$m,
Sentinel-2 capped to $10$m, as in \Cref{tab:resolution_ablation}), macro-average
PQ is $38.5$ for PlanetScope and $24.1$ for FTW-PRUE+ (B7) under the same recipe,
a $+14.4$\,point PlanetScope margin. Figure~\ref{fig:per_country_bars} breaks this
into per-region deltas and contrasts them with FTW's pixel-instance object F1.
PlanetScope wins on $22/23$ regions by polygon PQ but only $11/22$ by
pixel-instance object F1, so the pixel-instance metric understates the
resolution benefit shown by polygon metrics. The PQ win count is a
directional consistency check across regions, not an out-of-distribution
generalization test: under the PRUE protocol every region contributes both
train and test chips. All configurations use a single seed
(\texttt{seed\_everything}\,$=7$), so we report point estimates and do not
claim seed-level significance.

The largest gains are mid-size European regions; the only region that does not
favor PlanetScope is Portugal, where the two sensors tie ($-0.1$\,points).
Germany is the narrowest PlanetScope win ($+2.0$\,points overall, but $+4.6$ on
sub-$0.5$\,ha fields): the gain there is concentrated in small fields because the
Planet model recognizes and localizes Germany's large parcels less well than
S2-B7. The Planet model tends to \emph{merge} adjacent parcels rather than fragment them,
predicting about half as many polygons as there are parcels. Those boundaries
are often low-contrast cadastral lines rather than physical features, so the
resolution advantage is smallest exactly where the boundary is not a visible
edge.

\paragraph{Field-size breakdown.}
Per-region averages confound field size with geography. Binning
\emph{individual} GT fields by area resolves it, but the binning must be fair
across sensors. When comparing native sensor outputs, we score against the \emph{true}
FTW polygons (not the rasterized mask), with Sentinel-2 capped to $10$m and
PlanetScope at $3$m, so a $10$m model is not credited for sub-$10$m boundaries
painted by an upsampled output grid (\Cref{tab:resolution_ablation}). Scored this
way, PlanetScope leads at every size, and the relative gap is largest
on the smallest parcels: real $3$m roughly doubles Sentinel-2's small-field
polygon quality ($15.7$ vs $7.5$ PQ). Upsampling Sentinel-2 to a 512-pixel grid
recovers part of the small/medium gap ($7.5\!\to\!11.8$) but none of the large-field gap
($36.1$ vs native $37.7$, against PlanetScope's $52.0$)---large fields are
already resolvable at $10$m, so the remaining advantage is not explained by
output-grid resolution alone.
This is the quantity a deployed pipeline would produce: vectorized parcels from
each sensor's own resolution. It follows the same pattern as the
rasterization-only result in \Cref{tab:representation_ceiling}.

\subsection{Ablation summary}

The full lever-by-lever recipe sweep --- including the rejected clDice,
SDF, frame-field, CutMix, and curriculum variants --- is consolidated in
\Cref{tab:ablation_summary} (appendix). The largest effects are the
augmentation bundle and, among the rejected levers, the SDF auxiliary head
($-3.1$).

The main ablation result is that FTP needs stronger regularization than the S2
recipe: PRUE+ adds +4.5 Obj F1 over the lighter swap-order/gamma step, while
CutMix, clDice, frame fields, and curriculum boundary dilation do not improve
the final model. The SDF auxiliary head helps without augmentations but hurts
with PRUE+, suggesting that the auxiliary task is redundant once the model is
already regularized.

\section{Limitations and future work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Coverage and selection bias.} 7{,}800 (5.5\%) of (patch, window) targets
lack a PlanetScope companion for the requested area and seasonal window (net
yield $94.5\%$; \Cref{tab:scope}), mostly from activation failures or patches
with only low-quality imagery and no acceptable same-season replacement. Those
Planet windows are excluded from FTP rather than paired with a mismatched
PlanetScope scene. The dropped fraction is not uniform: retention is lowest in
cloudier countries (Portugal $24\%$, Finland $75\%$, the Netherlands $77\%$),
and dropped patches are systematically cloudier and \emph{lower} label-density
than kept ones. Reported numbers for those regions therefore reflect a clearer,
higher-density surviving subset, and the release under-represents low-density
scenes with poor image quality; full per-country cross-tabs ship with the index.

\textbf{Hard evaluation regions.} Kenya is the only presence-only FTW region, so
it is excluded from every supervised macro-average; a cleaner comparison would
also mask its unlabeled land during training. Portugal (terraced micro-fields near the PlanetScope GSD) is the one region
where PlanetScope does not pull ahead of Sentinel-2 (\Cref{sec:polygon-first}).
Cambodia remains difficult despite a PlanetScope win: its FTW v2 windows overlap only
monsoon-season coverage, giving the release's highest haze fraction and a
high-precision/low-recall model, which leaves Cambodia the narrowest object
recall among the regions PlanetScope wins.

\textbf{Temporal and spectral scope.} The PlanetScope date is the closest
cloud-free match to the FTW season midpoint (usually within two weeks), and FTP
ships the two FTW v2 seasonal windows per patch, so we make no multi-year or
cross-year claim. The release targets the 4-band Dove product; 8-band SuperDove
would add red-edge/yellow bands but postdates most FTW v2 windows.

\textbf{Societal impact.} Parcel-level field maps can be misused, for example for
farm-level surveillance or credit profiling of individual growers, which would
fall hardest on smallholders. We intend the labels and predictions for aggregate
monitoring rather than targeting individual farms.

\textbf{Future work.} Country-scale inference is the next practical test of FTP,
along with resolution-agnostic joint training over the Planet and Sentinel-2
versions of FTW.

\section{Conclusion}

Fields of the Planet (FTP) is a 3m PlanetScope companion to Fields of The
World that releases co-registered imagery, per-patch UDM2 quality statistics,
rasterized FTW labels, and the range-read pipeline that builds them. Under a
matched PRUE recipe, moving from 10m Sentinel-2 to 3m PlanetScope improves
polygon-level delineation measured by PQ, F1$_{[.5:.95]}$, and matched-polygon
meter-scale boundary error, with the gain concentrated on the sub-$0.5$\,ha fields that a 10m grid
cannot represent in the first place. The choice of metric changes the
conclusion: pixel IoU stays high while small parcels merge and fragment, and
pixel-instance object F1 ranks PlanetScope ahead of Sentinel-2 on only $11/22$
regions where polygon PQ does so on $22/23$. We therefore recommend object F1 and panoptic quality,
scored on vectorized parcels at each sensor's native resolution, as the default
metrics for field-boundary mapping.

\section{Polygon-metric definitions}
\label{app:metrics}

All object-level benchmark metrics are computed \emph{after vectorization}, on
the field-interior class only; pixel IoU is reported before vectorization as a
secondary continuity metric. This appendix gives the exact definitions
implemented in our evaluation code, so that every column of
\Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} is reproducible from this text alone.

\paragraph{Vectorization.}
For a patch, let the predicted field-interior mask be the set of its
4-connected components, each traced to a polygonal geometry, giving a predicted
polygon set $\mathcal{P}=\{p_1,\ldots,p_M\}$; the ground-truth polygons form
$\mathcal{G}=\{g_1,\ldots,g_N\}$. All set operations below use polygon
\emph{areas} (via exact geometry), not pixel counts.

\paragraph{Polygon IoU and matching.}
For a ground-truth/prediction pair the intersection-over-union is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{IoU}(g,p)=\frac{\mathrm{area}(g\cap p)}{\mathrm{area}(g\cup p)}
=\frac{\mathrm{area}(g\cap p)}{\mathrm{area}(g)+\mathrm{area}(p)-\mathrm{area}(g\cap p)}.
\end{equation}
At an IoU threshold $\tau$, a prediction is a true positive iff it overlaps a
ground-truth polygon with $\mathrm{IoU}>\tau$. Because polygons within each set
have disjoint interiors, for $\tau\ge 0.5$ a polygon can exceed $\tau$ with at
most one polygon of the other set, so the matching
$\mathcal{M}_\tau\subseteq\mathcal{G}\times\mathcal{P}$ is a well-defined
partial bijection (greedy and optimal matching coincide). Writing
$\mathrm{TP}_\tau=|\mathcal{M}_\tau|$, $\mathrm{FP}_\tau=M-\mathrm{TP}_\tau$,
and $\mathrm{FN}_\tau=N-\mathrm{TP}_\tau$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Prec}_\tau=\frac{\mathrm{TP}_\tau}{\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\mathrm{FP}_\tau},\qquad
\mathrm{Rec}_\tau=\frac{\mathrm{TP}_\tau}{\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\mathrm{FN}_\tau},\qquad
\mathrm{F1}_\tau=\frac{2\,\mathrm{TP}_\tau}{2\,\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\mathrm{FP}_\tau+\mathrm{FN}_\tau}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Recognition, segmentation, and panoptic quality~\cite{kirillov2019panoptic}.}
With the match set fixed at $\tau=0.5$,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RQ}=\mathrm{F1}_{0.5},\qquad
\mathrm{SQ}=\frac{1}{\mathrm{TP}_{0.5}}\!\!\sum_{(g,p)\in\mathcal{M}_{0.5}}\!\!\mathrm{IoU}(g,p),\qquad
\mathrm{PQ}=\mathrm{SQ}\times\mathrm{RQ}.
\end{equation}
If $\mathrm{TP}_{0.5}=0$, we set $\mathrm{SQ}=0$ and hence $\mathrm{PQ}=0$;
boundary error is undefined. Substituting otherwise shows this is the original
panoptic quality,
$\mathrm{PQ}=\big(\sum_{(g,p)\in\mathcal{M}_{0.5}}\mathrm{IoU}(g,p)\big)\big/\big(\mathrm{TP}_{0.5}+\tfrac12\mathrm{FP}_{0.5}+\tfrac12\mathrm{FN}_{0.5}\big)$,
since $\mathrm{F1}_\tau=\mathrm{TP}_\tau/(\mathrm{TP}_\tau+\tfrac12\mathrm{FP}_\tau+\tfrac12\mathrm{FN}_\tau)$.

\paragraph{Threshold-averaged F1.}
Using the COCO threshold grid
$\mathcal{T}=\{0.5,0.55,\ldots,0.95\}$ ($|\mathcal{T}|=10$)~\cite{lin2014coco},
but averaging F1 rather than precision-recall AP,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{F1}_{[.5:.95]}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}\sum_{\tau\in\mathcal{T}}\mathrm{F1}_\tau .
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Polygon-count error.}
Let $\bar{M}$ and $\bar{N}$ be the per-patch mean numbers of predicted and
ground-truth polygons within a country. Because raw count errors scale with
parcel density, \Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} reports the normalized count
discrepancy $|\bar{M}-\bar{N}|/\bar{N}$, then macro-averages across countries
($|\Delta N|/N$). It is a coarse over/under-segmentation check independent of
matching.

\paragraph{Symmetric boundary chamfer~\cite{cheng2021biou}.}
For each matched pair $(g,p)\in\mathcal{M}_{0.5}$, let $B_g$ and $B_p$ be the
one-pixel-thick boundary pixel sets of the two polygons. The symmetric
chamfer distance, in pixels, is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Ch}(g,p)=\frac{1}{2}\!\left(
\frac{1}{|B_g|}\sum_{x\in B_g}\min_{y\in B_p}\lVert x-y\rVert_2
\;+\;
\frac{1}{|B_p|}\sum_{y\in B_p}\min_{x\in B_g}\lVert y-x\rVert_2
\right),
\end{equation}
where each inner nearest-boundary distance is read from the Euclidean
distance transform of the opposing boundary. The boundary error in meters is
$\mathrm{Ch}(g,p)\cdot\text{GSD}$, using the evaluated grid's ground sample
distance (3\,m for PlanetScope, 10\,m for native Sentinel-2). We report its mean
and 95th percentile over all matched pairs; pairs with an empty boundary on
either side are excluded. Only matched pairs contribute, so the chamfer scores
edge precision for the fields that were found (the same scoping as SQ); missed
and hallucinated fields are accounted for by RQ.

\paragraph{Pixel IoU (secondary).}
For continuity with the released PRUE protocol we also report the pixel-level
Jaccard index of the field-interior class, $\mathrm{IoU}_\text{px}=
|\hat{Y}\cap Y|/|\hat{Y}\cup Y|$ over all field-interior pixels, computed
before vectorization. Unlike the metrics above it has no notion of individual
parcels.

\paragraph{Aggregation.}
$\mathrm{TP}/\mathrm{FP}/\mathrm{FN}$ counts and matched IoUs are pooled
across all patches \emph{within} a country before forming that country's
RQ, SQ, PQ, and $\mathrm{F1}_{[.5:.95]}$; the chamfer is averaged over that
country's matched pairs. \Cref{tab:polygon_metrics} reports the macro-average
across the ten dense-label held-out countries (presence-only Kenya excluded;
see \Cref{sec:limitations}). Where a country has no predicted polygons under a
given configuration (e.g.\ Portugal under the weaker S2 models), its boundary error is
undefined and that configuration's two boundary columns average the remaining
nine countries; every other column averages all ten.

\paragraph{Field-area breakdown (\Cref{tab:resolution_ablation}).}
To localize \emph{where} resolution helps, we report panoptic quality per
field-size bin. Each ground-truth polygon is assigned by its area $A$ to
\emph{small} ($A<0.5$\,ha), \emph{medium} ($0.5\le A<2$\,ha), or \emph{large}
($A\ge2$\,ha); the $2$\,ha cut is the standard smallholder ceiling~\cite{lowder2016farms}
and $0.5$\,ha splits the dominant sub-hectare mass (median field $=0.41$\,ha).
Areas are computed in each tile's UTM projection, which is metric and
grid-invariant, so a parcel falls in the same bin regardless of sensor (a
web-Mercator area would be inflated by up to $3.9\times$ at Swedish
latitudes). Matched true positives and false negatives are assigned by
ground-truth area; unmatched predicted polygons are counted as false positives
in the bin determined by their predicted area. \Cref{tab:resolution_ablation} reports
$\mathrm{PQ}=\mathrm{SQ}\times\mathrm{RQ}_{.5}$ per bin, macro-averaged over the
ten dense-label held-out countries and scored against the \emph{true} FTW
polygons under the protocol for each row (\Cref{sec:polygon-first}): native
$10$\,m Sentinel-2, the same model upsampled to a 512-pixel grid, and real $3$\,m
PlanetScope.
